## Post Template #2: Educational/Insight (Thought Leadership)

### Post Type: Text Post with Hook

```
Why do ALL your 1970s family photos look orange?

It's not a filter. It's chemistry.

In the 1970s, Kodak used a three-layer dye system:
→ Cyan (blue)
→ Magenta (red)
→ Yellow

The problem? Cyan was chemically unstable.

It fades faster than the other layers.

Without cyan to balance the colors, photos shift toward red/orange/magenta.

That's why nearly every 1970s color photo now looks like it was shot through an orange filter.

The good news: the information isn't gone.

Modern AI can analyze the fading pattern and mathematically reconstruct what the cyan layer SHOULD look like.

The results are often dramatic.

If you have 1970s photos that look "ruined" - they're probably not. The colors are hiding, not destroyed.

Try a free AI restoration tool and see for yourself.

#Photography #Science #AI #PhotoRestoration #1970s #TechHistory

## Post Template #5: Tips/How-To (Value-First)

### Post Type: List/Tips Format

```
5 mistakes people make when restoring old family photos:

1️⃣ NOT SAVING THE ORIGINAL
Always keep the unedited scan. Restoration should be non-destructive.

2️⃣ SCANNING TOO LOW
300 DPI isn't enough. Use 600 minimum, 1200 for damaged photos.

3️⃣ OVER-PROCESSING
More sharpening ≠ better. Subtle restoration looks natural.

4️⃣ SKIPPING METADATA
Write names and dates somewhere. Future generations won't know who's in the photo.

5️⃣ WAITING TOO LONG
Photos are still degrading. The elderly relatives who can identify faces won't wait forever.

The good news: AI tools have made the technical part easy.

The hard part? Actually starting.
