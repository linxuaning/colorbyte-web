## What Remini Is Actually Good At

Remini became popular because it can make faces look sharper in low-quality images. That is a real strength. If you have a relatively clean portrait where the main issue is softness, compression, or mild blur, Remini can produce a visible face enhancement.

That makes Remini useful for:

- Smartphone portraits that look low resolution
- Social media images with compression artifacts
- Clean scans where the face is the only weak area
- Quick mobile previews before deciding whether a photo is worth deeper work

For those cases, Remini's face-first design makes sense.

## Where Remini Falls Short on Old Photos

Old photo restoration is not just face sharpening. Historical prints often combine several problems at once:

- Yellowing or faded photographic paper
- Scratches across faces, clothing, or backgrounds
- Torn corners or missing edges
- Water stains, mold, foxing, or chemical marks
- Low scan resolution from small wallet-size prints
- Color shift from aged film or prints

Remini is not built around that full damage profile. It may make the central face look sharper while leaving the rest of the photograph visibly damaged. In some cases, that creates an odd result: a modern-looking face inside an old, still-damaged print.

## Can Remini Repair Scratches or Tears?

Not reliably. Scratch and tear repair requires a model that understands damaged image regions and reconstructs surrounding content. That is closer to inpainting and restoration than portrait enhancement.

If a scratch crosses a cheek or eye, Remini may smooth or hallucinate some face detail. But if the scratch crosses a suit, a background, a handwritten border, or a torn paper edge, it does not have enough restoration-specific context to handle the whole image consistently.

For family archive work, consistency matters. The goal is not just a sharper face; it is a photograph that still feels like the original, only restored.
