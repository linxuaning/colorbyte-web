The American disability rights movement, which produced the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in 1990, generated a distinctive photographic record of activists demanding inclusion in American public life. Photographs from disability rights demonstrations — including the famous Capitol Crawl of 1990 where activists with disabilities climbed the Capitol steps to press for passage of the ADA — document a civil rights struggle that transformed American public space and law.

## Disability Rights Heritage

Disability rights photographs document the specific activities of the movement: the demonstrations and direct actions through which activists with disabilities made their exclusion from public life visible, the organizing meetings of disability rights organizations, the legal advocacy work that built toward the ADA, and the community building that sustained activists across years of organizing. These photographs show people with disabilities as political actors demanding their rights rather than as passive recipients of charitable concern.

## Movement Documentation

Disability rights movement photographs face preservation challenges related to their activist contexts and to the specific circumstances of activists with disabilities who faced additional challenges in maintaining archival collections. AI restoration addresses these preservation challenges, recovering the visual documentation of a civil rights movement that achieved significant legislative victories.
