# Restoring 1960s Hippie Commune and Intentional Community Photos

The hippie commune photographs from the late 1960s and 1970s document some of the most deliberately alternative community experiments in American history. The intentional communities — from the famous ones like The Farm in Tennessee to thousands of smaller experiments — were photographed by their participants.

## The Commune as Alternative Archive

Commune photographs document communities that deliberately positioned themselves outside mainstream American life. The specific practices — communal work, shared resources, alternative relationship structures, back-to-land agriculture — are visible in photographs that document daily community life.

## Photography as Community Memory

Intentional communities often had a collective approach to documentation. The commune photographs were community property as much as personal property — shared records of shared experience. This communal approach to photography produces a different kind of archive than conventional family photography.

## The Long Arc of the Commune

Many commune photographs document not just the founding idealism but the arc of community life — the challenges, the successes, the departures. For people who lived in communes and are now in their sixties and seventies, restoration of these photographs connects them to the most communally lived period of their lives.
