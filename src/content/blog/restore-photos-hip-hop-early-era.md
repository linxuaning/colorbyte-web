Hip-hop culture — born in the Bronx in the mid-1970s and documented photographically from its earliest years — has become one of the most globally significant American cultural exports. Family photographs connected to early hip-hop communities document the South Bronx origins of a cultural movement that would transform American popular music and global youth culture. These photographs — of block parties, breaking battles, graffiti art, and the communities where hip-hop developed — are primary sources for understanding the culture's origins.

## Cultural Origins

Early hip-hop photographs have a specific documentary character reflecting both the photography technology available to working-class New York communities in the late 1970s-early 1980s and the organic, community-based nature of the culture's early development. The photographs of block parties in the South Bronx, the breaking ciphers in parks and community centers, and the graffiti-covered train cars that carried hip-hop visual culture across New York City constitute a visual archive of extraordinary cultural historical significance.

## Community Documentation

Early hip-hop photographs face preservation challenges related to their origins in communities with limited access to archival resources, the informal contexts in which many photographs were taken and stored, and the 40-50 years of aging that these photographs have experienced.
