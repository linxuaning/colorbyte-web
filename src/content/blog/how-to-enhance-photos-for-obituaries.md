When a loved one passes, families often find themselves searching through shoeboxes, old phones, and social media profiles looking for photos — any photo — that can represent the person's life. What they find is rarely perfect: a faded snapshot from a family gathering, a cropped image pulled from a group photo, a scan of an old print that's been folded and stored for decades.

These imperfect photos can be made usable. AI photo restoration and enhancement tools have made it possible to take damaged, blurry, or low-resolution family photos and turn them into clear, printable tributes worthy of any memorial context.

## What Photo Qualities Matter Most for Obituaries and Memorial Programs?

Memorial photos serve different purposes than typical personal photos:

- **Print resolution**: Obituaries, programs, and tribute boards require specific DPI minimums that casual digital photos often don't meet
- **Clarity and sharpness**: The photo should be recognizably the person — blurry or heavily degraded images don't serve this purpose
- **Dignity**: Photos that look restored and clean honor the person; visibly damaged photos call attention to the damage rather than the subject
- **Appropriate expression**: The photo should reflect the person as family and friends knew them

AI tools can address the first three directly. The fourth is a family decision — but having a repaired, high-resolution version of the best available photo gives families more options than they'd have otherwise.

## How Do You Repair Torn or Faded Family Photos for Memorial Use?

The most common challenge families face is working with old printed photographs that have physical damage: tears from handling, water stains from flooding or humidity, fading from sunlight exposure, creases from decades of storage, and the brown spots called foxing that develop from oxidation.

The [Old Photo Restoration](/old-photo-restoration) tool addresses all of these simultaneously. The AI works patch by patch across the image, identifying damage regions and reconstructing them from surrounding context. For portraits — which most memorial photos are — the model has strong learned priors for what faces look like and handles facial reconstruction with particular care.

The process takes under two minutes and produces a downloadable file that can be used directly for print or digital submission without additional editing.

## How Do You Make a Small Photo Large Enough to Print?

Families who find photos on social media or in old digital files often have images that are far too small for print use. A profile photo from Facebook is typically 170×170 pixels — impossibly small for a print obituary. A photo screenshot from a messaging app might be 400×600 pixels, which prints sharply at only about 1.3×2 inches at 300 DPI.

The [Photo Enhancer](/photo-enhancer) uses Real-ESRGAN upscaling to increase these small images to print-ready sizes. Unlike simple interpolation — which just stretches pixels and produces a blurry, pixelated result — Real-ESRGAN generates fine detail at higher resolutions based on patterns learned from millions of image pairs. A 400×600 photo can be enhanced to 1600×2400 or larger with realistic sharpness and detail.

For program photos (typically 2×3 or 3×4 inches at 300 DPI) and standard obituary photos, this upscaling process produces acceptable print quality from surprisingly small source images.

## How Do You Fix a Photo Taken as a Screenshot or Low-Quality Scan?

Photos pulled from social media, messaging apps, or older websites are typically heavily compressed. JPEG compression creates blocky, mosaic-like artifacts that are especially visible in smooth areas like skin and sky. Multiple rounds of save-download-repost compound these artifacts.

The [JPEG Artifact Remover](/jpeg-artifact-remover) uses SwinIR to clean these artifacts before enhancement, producing a smoother source image that the upscaling model can work with more effectively. For photos that have been shared many times digitally, this pre-cleaning step makes a visible difference in the final output quality.

## What If the Best Available Portrait Is Blurry or Soft?

Old photos are often blurry — either because they were taken with a camera that struggled to focus, or because they were the out-of-focus background member of a group shot who needs to be cropped and used as a solo image.
