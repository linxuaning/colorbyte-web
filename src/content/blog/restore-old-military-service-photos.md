You inherit a military service portrait from a great-grandparent who served in WWII. The print has yellowed, the face has softened, and you're not sure what unit information is visible. You want to restore it to a state worth framing for your family's history record.

This guide walks through restoring old military service photographs with attention to historical accuracy and family genealogy integration.

## Why military service photos are good restoration candidates

Military photography in the 1900s-1990s had several characteristics that make these photos excellent AI restoration candidates:

- **Studio quality**: Most service portraits were taken by professional military photographers
- **Controlled lighting**: Studio strobes and consistent setup
- **Neutral backgrounds**: Plain backdrops focus attention on subject
- **Formal pose**: Standard posing reveals face clearly
- **High-resolution originals**: Military photos often used larger formats than civilian snapshots

These factors mean even severely faded service photos retain underlying detail that AI can recover.

## Step 1: Identify era and service branch

Before scanning, identify:
- **Era**: WWI (1914-1918), Interwar (1919-1939), WWII (1939-1945), Korea (1950-1953), Vietnam (1955-1975), Modern (1975+)
- **Service branch**: Army, Navy, Marines, Air Force (post-1947), Coast Guard
- **Visible rank/insignia**: Even partially visible
- **Unit patches**: Often shoulder or chest area

This identification informs:
- Approximate photo date (uniform style changes)
- What unit information you may research later
- Genealogical context (deployments, postings)

## Step 2: High-resolution scanning

Service photos benefit particularly from high-resolution scanning because:
- Unit insignia detail (often small)
- Rank chevrons and stripes
- Medal ribbons (visible on dress uniforms)
- Studio name (often printed on mount)

Scan settings:
- 1200+ DPI for standard 4×6 service portraits
- 2400+ DPI for small wallet-size or contact prints
- 16-bit color mode (preserves uniform color tones)
- Save unmodified scan as canonical

## Step 4: Cross-reference military archives

Once details are visible after AI restoration, research:

**Free resources**:
- **NARA** ([National Archives](https://www.archives.gov/research/military)) — comprehensive US military records
- **VA records** — Veterans Affairs archives
- **Find A Grave** — for deceased veterans, includes service information
- **State archives** — many states maintain state-level service records

**Subscription resources**:
- **Fold3** (Ancestry) — extensive military records, unit histories, casualty lists
- **MyHeritage** — military records integrated with family trees
- **Newspapers.com** — local newspaper coverage of service members

Cross-reference visible details (rank, unit, era) with these archives to build the service member's story.

## Step 5: Annotate restored photos

For genealogy archives, accompany the restored photo with verified information:
- Name (full name with middle initial)
- Rank at time of photo
- Unit (regiment, division, ship name, squadron)
- Dates of service
- Posting locations
- Decorations (verified from records)

This annotation transforms a "great-grandfather's WWII photo" into a documented family heritage record.

## Step 6: Decide about colorization

For B&W service photos:

**Arguments to keep B&W**:
- Uniform colors have specific historical references (AI may guess wrong)
- Genealogy archives prefer canonical B&W
- Aesthetic of era is part of the historical record

**Arguments to colorize**:
- Younger relatives connect more with color version
- Family display impact
- Some uniforms (dress whites, dress blues) are visually striking in color

If colorizing:
- Research uniform color charts for the specific era and branch first
- Use AI as starting point, manually correct uniform colors using historical references
- Label as "AI-interpreted color, manually corrected to era specifications"

## Special case: WWII era specifics

WWII service photos (1939-1945) have particular considerations:

- **V-Mail**: Service photos sometimes were V-mailed home, creating duplicate copies of varying quality
- **Combat conditions**: Field photos may have unusual damage from wartime storage
- **Group/unit photos**: Often have many faces; AI restores closest faces best, dimishing returns toward background
- **Military photographer credit**: Some prints have "Signal Corps" or "USMC Combat Camera" markings — these are historical attribution worth preserving

## Special case: Vietnam-era specifics

Vietnam-era photos (1955-1975) have:
- More color photography (Kodachrome era)
- Field condition damage (humidity, rough handling)
- Sometimes deliberately poor quality (combat zone photos)

Color Vietnam photos often show magenta shift from age — AI color correction handles this dramatically.

## Realistic expectations

Service photo restoration outcomes:
- **Studio service portrait + light fade**: AI produces excellent results, often near-perfect
- **Group/unit photo + moderate fade**: Strong improvement on close faces, modest on background
- **Field/combat photo + damage**: Variable; manual cleanup may be required
- **Severely degraded photo**: Limited recovery; consider professional conservator
