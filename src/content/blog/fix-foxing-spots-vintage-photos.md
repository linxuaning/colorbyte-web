## What Is Foxing?

Foxing refers to the scattered brown or reddish-brown spots that appear on paper and photographs over time. Despite decades of study, the exact cause remains somewhat debated among conservators.

### Causes of Foxing

**Fungal Growth Theory**:
- Microscopic fungi colonize paper fibers
- Thrive in humid environments
- Produce brown metabolic byproducts
- Often accompanied by musty smell
- May be active or dormant

**Chemical Oxidation Theory**:
- Iron particles in paper oxidize
- Impurities react with atmospheric moisture
- Creates localized rust-like staining
- More common in older paper types
- Can occur without biological involvement

**Combined Factors**:
Most experts now believe foxing results from multiple factors:
- Paper composition and impurities
- Environmental conditions (humidity, temperature)
- Possible fungal or bacterial activity
- Age and storage conditions
- Chemical reactions with pollutants

### Identifying Foxing vs. Other Damage

| Characteristic | Foxing | Mold/Mildew | Water Stains | Age Spots |
|---------------|--------|-------------|--------------|-----------|
| Color | Reddish-brown | Gray/black/green | Brown rings | Yellow-brown |
| Pattern | Scattered spots | Fuzzy patches | Defined edges | Random blotches |
| Texture | Flat, same as paper | Raised, fuzzy | May be raised | Flat |
| Location | Random across surface | Often edges/corners | Tide lines visible | Concentrated areas |
| Smell | Slight musty | Strong mildew | May smell damp | Usually odorless |

## Physical Treatment Options

Physical removal of foxing is challenging and often best left to professional conservators. However, some approaches may help.

### Environmental Stabilization

Before any treatment, stop active deterioration:

**Control Humidity**:
- Ideal range: 30-40% relative humidity
- Use silica gel packets in storage
- Avoid basements and attics
- Monitor with hygrometer
- Address any moisture sources

**Improve Air Circulation**:
- Don't stack photos tightly
- Use archival sleeves with ventilation
- Periodically air out storage boxes
- Consider activated charcoal absorbers

### Surface Cleaning

For photos with surface contamination:

1. **Wear nitrile gloves** to prevent oil transfer
2. **Use soft brush** to remove loose particles
3. **Try soft eraser** on non-image areas only (test first)
4. **Never scrub** foxing spots directly
5. **Avoid moisture** which can reactivate fungi

### Professional Conservation

Professional treatment may include:

**Washing and Bleaching**:
- Carefully controlled aqueous treatment
- Reduces but rarely eliminates foxing
- Risk of image damage
- Expensive and time-consuming

**Chemical Treatment**:
- Ethanol/water solutions
- Calcium hydroxide deacidification
- Enzyme treatments for biological foxing
- Requires expertise and equipment

**When to Seek Professional Help**:
- Historically significant photographs
- Active mold growth
- Severe widespread foxing
- Valuable prints or negatives
- Photos showing rapid deterioration

## Long-Term Prevention

Protect your photographs from developing foxing in the future.

### Proper Storage Materials

**Use Acid-Free Materials**:
- Archival boxes and folders
- Acid-free tissue paper interleaving
- Buffered storage materials
- Avoid cardboard and newsprint

**Appropriate Enclosures**:
- Polypropylene sleeves (clear)
- Polyester photo pages
- Acid-free paper envelopes
- Never PVC or vinyl

### Environmental Control

**Ideal Conditions**:
- Temperature: 65-70°F (18-21°C)
- Relative Humidity: 30-40%
- Stable conditions (no fluctuation)
- Away from light sources
- Good air circulation

**Avoid**:
- Basements (moisture)
- Attics (temperature extremes)
- Near exterior walls
- Close to heating/cooling vents
- Direct sunlight exposure

### Regular Monitoring

- Check stored photos every 6-12 months
- Look for new spots or spreading
- Monitor for musty odors
- Replace degraded storage materials
- Address problems immediately

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can foxing spots be completely removed from photographs?**

Short answer: Physically, foxing is very difficult to remove without damaging the photo. Professional conservation can reduce visibility but rarely eliminates it entirely. Digital restoration, however, can completely remove foxing spots, making photos look pristine. Our [AI restoration tool](/enhance-old-photos) handles foxing effectively.

**Is foxing contagious—will it spread to other photos?**

Short answer: If foxing is caused by fungal growth, it can potentially spread to nearby photos stored in similar conditions. Isolate affected photos, improve storage conditions (lower humidity), and consider using separate acid-free enclosures. Digital copies provide perfect backup regardless.

**Will foxing continue to get worse over time?**

Short answer: Yes, without intervention. Foxing typically worsens in high-humidity environments. Improving storage conditions can slow or halt progression. Creating digital backups ensures you preserve the image even if physical deterioration continues.

**Can I bleach foxing spots out of old photos?**

Short answer: Never attempt bleaching at home—it will likely destroy your photo. Professional conservators sometimes use controlled chemical treatments, but results are unpredictable. Digital restoration is safer, more effective, and completely reversible.

**What causes the musty smell on photos with foxing?**

Short answer: The musty odor indicates fungal activity. The fungi producing foxing spots also release volatile compounds that create that characteristic old-paper smell. Proper storage with good ventilation helps reduce odors and slow fungal growth.
