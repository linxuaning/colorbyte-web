## What Happens to Photos When They Get Wet?

Understanding the damage helps you understand what's recoverable. A photograph is built in layers: a paper or plastic base, a binding layer, and a thin emulsion coating that holds the actual image dyes or silver crystals. When water penetrates these layers:

- **Emulsion swells and softens**, making the surface tacky and vulnerable to tearing or sticking to other surfaces
- **Dyes migrate**, causing color bleeding that smears reds, blues, and yellows across adjacent areas
- **Paper bases warp and buckle**, distorting the geometry of the image
- **Tannins and minerals in the water deposit as stains**, leaving tide marks even after the photo dries
- **Mold colonizes the emulsion**, producing fuzzy white or green patches that physically destroy the image layer

The good news: with careful physical handling followed by digital restoration, most of this damage is reversible or at least significantly improvable.

## How Do You Rescue Wet Photos Right Now?

The first 24 hours are critical. Here's what to do immediately:

**Separate gently.** If photos are stuck together, float the bundle in clean, room-temperature water — not hot — for 20–30 minutes to re-soften the emulsion bond. Start peeling from one corner, slowly. Never force it.

**Dry face-up.** Lay each print face-up on a clean paper towel or lint-free cloth. Avoid direct sunlight and heat sources like hair dryers, which can permanently curl the emulsion.

**Freeze what you can't handle.** If you have more wet prints than time allows, seal groups in plastic bags and put them in the freezer. Freezing halts mold growth completely and buys you weeks to work through them systematically.

**Scan as soon as dry.** Even a damaged scan at 600 DPI captures far more recoverable information than a smartphone photo. The more data you capture, the better the AI restoration will be.
