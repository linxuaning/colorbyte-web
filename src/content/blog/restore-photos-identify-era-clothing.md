The photograph has no caption, no date, no studio mark. The subjects are unidentified. But the photograph is not undated — it dates itself, in the clothing every person is wearing.

Dating photographs through clothing analysis is a genuine skill used by archivists, historians, and genealogists. For restoration purposes, understanding when a photograph was taken improves the restoration decisions — the expected tonal palette, the likely photographic format, the appropriate level of sharpening.

## Clothing as Chronological Evidence

**Women's hemlines** changed dramatically across the 20th century and can date a photograph within 5-10 years:
- Pre-1920: floor-length
- 1920s: knee-length (shocking at the time)
- 1930s: mid-calf (economic modesty)
- 1940s: knee-length (wartime practicality)
- 1950s: full skirts, varied lengths
- 1960s: dramatically shorter (mini skirt era)
- Each subsequent decade follows recognizable patterns

**Men's lapels** on suits have varied width across the decades:
- 1930s-1950s: moderately wide
- 1960s: narrowing
- 1970s: extremely wide
- 1980s: narrowing again

**Collar styles, tie widths, hairstyles** — all carry era-specific information.

## Using Clothing Evidence for Restoration

When you can identify the approximate decade of a photograph:

**Expected photographic characteristics inform restoration parameters.** A photograph from 1928 should be silver gelatin, neutral to slightly warm in tone. A photograph from 1973 should be Kodacolor with cyan dye shift. Understanding which format you're working with helps set the correct restoration targets.

**Color choices** for colorization (if requested) can be informed by the era's fashion conventions. Knowing that a dress was photographed in the 1950s helps the colorization AI make more appropriate color choices.

**Grain and sharpness expectations** match the format — a 1910 large-format studio portrait should have different characteristics than a 1968 Instamatic snapshot.
