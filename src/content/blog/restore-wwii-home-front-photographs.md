# Restoring WWII Home Front Photographs

Eleanor had kept her victory garden photographs in a box labeled "The War" — a label that contained multitudes. There was the garden itself, photographed in stages from the bare lot in spring 1942 to the abundant harvest of fall 1944. There were the rationing books, fanned out for the camera as though they were something to be proud of (they were). There was a photograph of her mother standing next to a car with a red sticker on the windshield — a C-sticker, which meant unlimited gas rationing, which meant her father was doing essential war work.

The photographs were a social history as much as a family history. They showed what America looked like when an entire country decided to do the same thing at the same time.

## The Snapshot Aesthetic of the Home Front

WWII home front photographs were predominantly amateur snapshots. The Kodak Brownie and similar box cameras were everywhere. The film was black-and-white — color film existed but was expensive and in short supply. The processing was done at drugstores and camera shops, with varying quality.

This combination — amateur cameras, variable processing — produces photographs that range from technically excellent to barely salvageable. The best ones have good shadow detail, clear faces, and reasonable sharpness. The worst have flat contrast, chemical spotting, and faces reduced to pale blurs.

## Specific Damage in 1940s Snapshots

**Fading in the highlights** is nearly universal. The bright areas of 1940s amateur prints — sky, skin in sunlight, white clothing — have often lost detail entirely, going to a uniform pale gray or cream.

**Chemical spotting** from inconsistent processing appears as small, irregular spots scattered across the image. These are distinct from the more regular pattern of foxing and easier to remove digitally.

**Print curl** from 80 years of humidity cycling has often cracked the emulsion in curved photos. Scan carefully — a curved print creates focus problems at the edges.

## Restoring Context Along with Images

Home front photographs often have handwritten dates and captions on the back — the only documentation of what you're looking at. Before restoration, photograph or scan the back of every print. This context is irreplaceable.

Eleanor's victory garden photographs came out well. The faces were sharp enough for confident identification, and the garden itself — beans, tomatoes, squash — was legible enough to read. She made a small book for her grandchildren: America in wartime, as seen through two years of amateur photography and one family's commitment to doing their part.
