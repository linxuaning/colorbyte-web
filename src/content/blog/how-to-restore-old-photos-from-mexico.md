Mexico's photographic tradition is one of the most vibrant and historically significant in the Americas. Beginning with the daguerreotype studios established in Mexico City in the 1840s and accelerating through the extraordinary visual documentation of the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920), Mexican photography developed an aesthetic sensibility that has influenced global visual culture far beyond what its recognition in mainstream photo history would suggest. For Mexican families today — whether living in Mexico or as part of the vast Mexican diaspora in the United States and beyond — old photographs are essential links to family histories shaped by revolution, migration, land, and community.

## What Is the Casasola Archive and Why Does It Matter for Family Photo Restoration?

The Casasola Archive, assembled by photographer Agustín Víctor Casasola and his family, represents one of the most significant documentary photographic collections in Latin American history. Containing over half a million negatives and prints documenting the Mexican Revolution and early 20th-century Mexican society, the Casasola aesthetic — dramatic contrast, documentary directness, authentic presence of subjects — established visual conventions for Mexican photography that influenced studio and vernacular photography for decades afterward.

For families restoring photographs from this era, the Casasola aesthetic provides important context: photographs from the 1910s and 1920s often feature the high-contrast, sharp-focus gelatin silver printing style that was standard in Mexican studios of the period. This style ages well in some respects (the high contrast preserves edge definition even as prints yellow) but is vulnerable to silver mirroring, which can completely obliterate highlight detail. ## How Did Indigenous Community Portrait Photography Develop in Mexico?

Indigenous community portrait photography in Mexico has a complex and layered history. During the colonial and early national periods, Indigenous subjects were often photographed by mestizo or European photographers as subjects of ethnographic documentation rather than as clients seeking personal portraits. By the late 19th century, this had begun to shift: some Indigenous communities in Oaxaca, Chiapas, Yucatán, and other regions developed their own relationships with local photography studios, and formal portraits became integrated into important community ceremonies and events.

The tradition of photographing important community events — festivals, religious ceremonies, agricultural rituals — created an extensive archive of community life that is now invaluable for cultural documentation. Many of these photographs were taken on medium-format cameras by itinerant photographers who traveled between communities, and the prints were often stored in collective settings (community houses, churches, or with village elders) rather than in individual family archives. Photographs from these community archives that have survived often show severe environmental damage from storage in uncontrolled conditions.

## What Damage Patterns Are Most Common in Mexican Historical Photographs?

Mexican historical photographs show damage patterns shaped by the country's climate diversity. In tropical coastal regions (Veracruz, Tabasco, the Yucatán Peninsula), high humidity causes mold growth and foxing of extraordinary severity — photographs stored in these regions for 50 years may show mold damage across 40% or more of the image surface. In highland regions (Mexico City, Oaxaca, Guadalajara), the drier climate is more conducive to long-term preservation, but earthquake damage has destroyed numerous family archives, particularly in 1985 (Mexico City), 1999 (Oaxaca), and 2017 (Mexico City and Puebla).

Water damage from flooding — including the periodic flooding that affects low-lying areas across Mexico — has compromised many family archives. Water-damaged photographs typically show tide marks (the characteristic wavy stain lines left by receding floodwater), silver sulfide staining, and emulsion loss in the most severely affected areas.
