# Restoring Photos Damaged by Cigarette Smoke

For families where someone smoked indoors for decades, photographs displayed on walls or kept in albums throughout the home may show distinctive yellowing and brown discoloration from cigarette smoke deposits. This nicotine and tar staining adds a yellowish-brown cast that can be mistaken for simple age-related fading but has specific characteristics. AI restoration can effectively address this type of damage.

## How Cigarette Smoke Affects Photographs

Cigarette smoke contains numerous chemical compounds, but the most photographically relevant are the particulate tars and nicotine that deposit on surfaces throughout a room. Over years of exposure, these deposits build up on photograph surfaces, emulsion layers, and paper supports, creating a yellow-brown cast. Unlike age-related fading (which attacks the photographic materials from within), smoke damage is primarily a surface deposit — the photographic image itself may be in good condition beneath the smoke layer. This is actually good news for restoration: the original image is largely intact, just obscured.

## Physical Cleaning Options

Unlike some types of photographic damage, surface smoke deposits can sometimes be partially removed physically before scanning. A professional conservator may attempt gentle surface cleaning with appropriate solvents to remove the worst of the deposit without disturbing the emulsion. For home treatment, the safest approach is to scan the photograph as-is rather than attempting physical cleaning, which risks damaging the emulsion layer. The AI restoration process can address the smoke staining in the digital domain more safely than physical cleaning in most cases.

## Digital Correction for Smoke Discoloration

AI restoration for smoke-damaged photographs treats the discoloration as a color cast correction problem — identifying the yellow-brown bias introduced by the smoke deposits and removing it to reveal the original color balance. For black-and-white photographs, the correction is simpler: removing the yellow cast restores the neutral gray tones. For color photographs, the correction needs to account for how the smoke has affected each color channel differently. Well-executed restoration of smoke-damaged photographs often produces dramatically improved results, revealing color accuracy that had been completely obscured by decades of smoke exposure.
