Photographs from African American families in the segregated South document lives lived within a system of legal discrimination — and lives that maintained dignity, community, and joy within those constraints. These photographs are personal family history and civil rights history simultaneously.

## The Black South Archive

African American families in the South created their own photographic record: the professional portrait studios in Black business districts that served communities excluded from white studios, the church photographs that documented community institutions, the family photographs that maintained connection and documented achievement in the face of systematic exclusion.

## Segregation Made Visible

The specific physical evidence of segregation — the separate facilities, the specific geography of Black neighborhoods, the churches and schools that served as community centers in the absence of access to white institutions — is visible in family photographs from this era.

## Documentation of Resilience

Jim Crow era photographs of African American families document not just the constraints of the system but the community structures — the Black business districts, the churches, the schools, the mutual aid organizations — that maintained community life within those constraints.
