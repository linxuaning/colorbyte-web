College Greek life — sororities and fraternities — has been a significant social institution in American higher education since the 19th century. Photographs from Greek life document both the specific social world of college fraternities and sororities and the broader college social culture of different eras. For alumni with strong Greek connections, these photographs document formative social relationships and community experiences that often persisted well beyond graduation.

## Greek Life Heritage

Greek life photographs document the specific activities and rituals of fraternal and sororal college life: rush activities, formal initiation ceremonies (within the bounds of what was permissible to photograph), the formal annual events (formals, founder's days, alumni weekends), and the informal social life of chapter houses. For sororities and fraternities with specific philanthropic or civic missions, photographs also document the community service activities through which chapters connected their social world to broader civic purposes.

## Social Documentation

Greek life photographs face preservation challenges related to the social contexts of their creation: photographs taken at parties, formal events, and informal chapter house gatherings were often informal in both creation and storage. The transition of chapters through many generations of student membership created archival discontinuity that resulted in photographs being dispersed rather than systematically preserved.
