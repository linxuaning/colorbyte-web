# How Long Does AI Photo Restoration Take?

One of the most common questions people have about AI photo restoration is simply: how long does it take? The answer depends on several factors — the complexity of the damage, the resolution of the image, and the tool you're using. But the short version is that AI restoration is dramatically faster than traditional manual methods, with most photos processed in seconds to a few minutes.

## Processing Time by Damage Complexity

Simple restoration jobs — a mildly faded color photo with minor surface scratches — typically process in 5–15 seconds with a modern AI tool. Moderately damaged photos with significant creasing, water staining, or color loss take 15–45 seconds as the model works through more complex reconstruction. Severely damaged images with missing sections, heavy mold damage, or extensive physical tearing require the most computation — typically 1–3 minutes. For comparison, a professional Photoshop restorer might spend 2–8 hours on a severely damaged photo that AI processes in under 3 minutes.

## Upload and Download Factors

Beyond the AI processing itself, total turnaround time includes upload time (dependent on your internet connection and file size), a processing queue wait if the service is busy, and download time for the finished result. High-resolution scans at 1200 DPI can be 50–100MB files — at typical home broadband speeds, uploading takes 30–90 seconds. PhotoFix optimizes its processing pipeline to minimize queue wait times, so total time from upload to downloadable result is typically under 2 minutes for most photos.

## Batch Processing for Multiple Photos

When you have a large collection of photos to restore — say, 50 images from a parent's photo albums — the math of per-photo processing time adds up. Even at 2 minutes per photo, 50 photos represent nearly 2 hours of sequential processing. PhotoFix supports batch uploading, allowing multiple photos to be processed in parallel, which dramatically reduces total wall-clock time for large collections. For families tackling a major digitization project, planning a batch session rather than processing photos one at a time is the most time-efficient approach.

## Practical Steps to Get Started

Before uploading your photo, take a moment to gently clean the surface with a soft, dry cloth to remove loose dust or debris. Scan at the highest resolution your equipment allows — 600 DPI is a solid baseline, but 1200 DPI or higher yields noticeably better restoration results. Save the scan as a TIFF or PNG rather than JPEG to preserve every detail.
