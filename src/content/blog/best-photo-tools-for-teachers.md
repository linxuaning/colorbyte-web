## What Problems Do Teachers Face with Photo Quality?

**Low-resolution historical images**: Public domain archives, library digitization projects, and museum image banks often provide photographs at relatively low resolution — suitable for web display but blurry when projected on a classroom screen or printed at handout size. Running these images through AI upscaling produces versions that hold up under projection and in print.

**Faded and damaged primary sources**: A photograph from the 1880s through 1940s used as a history classroom primary source is likely faded, scratched, or both. The damage reduces the detail that makes the photograph historically valuable — the faces, the environment, the material culture visible in the scene.

**Student family photos in poor condition**: Family history projects require students to bring in photographs of relatives. These are often decades old, in poor condition, and barely legible. AI restoration can make them usable for presentations and archives.

**Blurry field and activity photos**: Teachers documenting classroom activities, field trips, and student projects with smartphones or basic cameras often end up with soft, poorly-lit images that do not represent the quality of the work or experience being documented.

## The AI Models Behind the Tools

ArtImageHub's restoration pipeline uses peer-reviewed AI models:

- **NAFNet** (He et al., 2022) — deblurring and noise reduction, recovering sharpness in soft or motion-blurred images
- **Real-ESRGAN** (Wang et al., 2021) — AI upscaling trained on photographic textures, increasing resolution without the softening of traditional interpolation
- **SwinIR** (Liang et al., 2021) — detail and texture recovery from compressed or degraded images
- **CodeFormer + GFPGAN** — dedicated face restoration for historical portraits, recovering facial features that age and damage have obscured
- **DDColor** — AI colorization for black-and-white photographs using scene-aware color assignment

These are not simple filters — they are neural networks trained on millions of photographic images and peer-reviewed in academic literature. For classroom use, the quality is consistently strong enough for projected and printed educational materials.
