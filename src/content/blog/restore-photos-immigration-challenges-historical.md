# Restoring Photos That Document Immigration Challenges

Immigration to America has never been an unqualifiedly welcoming experience for all groups. Photographs that document the specific challenges faced by immigrant communities — the housing conditions of early immigrants, the labor exploitation, the expressions of nativist sentiment, the legal restrictions on specific ethnic groups, the community responses to discrimination — are important primary sources for understanding the full complexity of the immigrant experience. These photographs deserve preservation as historical documentation of a more complete American story.

## Tenement and Urban Poverty Photography

Jacob Riis's documentation of New York tenement conditions in the 1880s–1890s is the most famous example of social documentary photography of immigrant poverty, but similar documentation was produced in cities across the country by settlement house workers, Progressive Era reformers, and occasionally by immigrants themselves. Family photographs from early immigrant communities sometimes inadvertently document the crowded, difficult housing conditions of early immigrant urban life — the small rooms, the multiple families sharing space, the improvised domestic arrangements of people who arrived with few resources.

## Legal Discrimination and Its Visual Documentation

Some immigrant communities faced specific legal discrimination that generated distinctive photographic documentation: the exclusion-era identification papers that Chinese Americans were required to carry, the internment camp photographs of Japanese Americans, the photographs of 'Colored' and 'White' signs that documented racial segregation's impact on communities of color throughout the South. These photographs, when they exist in family archives, are primary sources for understanding the specific legal barriers that specific communities faced in American history.

## Community Resilience and Self-Documentation

In response to the limitations and hostilities of mainstream documentation, immigrant communities often developed their own photographic traditions — community photographers who documented community life from an insider perspective, community newspapers that published photographs of community events and achievements, and community organizations that maintained photographic archives of their own history. These self-generated community photographs present immigrant communities from their own perspective rather than as subjects of outside documentation. Preserving and restoring these community self-portraits is an act of affirming the community's own historical agency.
