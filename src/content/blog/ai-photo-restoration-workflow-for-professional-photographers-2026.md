Adding photo restoration as a paid service has become one of the most reliable ways for portrait, wedding, and event photographers to keep clients on the books between shoots. The work is quiet, profitable, and — since the latest generation of AI restoration tools landed in 2025 — almost entirely scalable from a single workstation.

This guide is the workflow I've helped a dozen studios put in place over the last eighteen months. It assumes you already shoot professionally, you already have client trust, and you want to add restoration without hiring a retoucher or buying a $4,000 lab license. The exact tools below get re-tested each quarter; the steps don't change.

## The Photographer's Restoration Funnel — Why It Works

Most studios stumble into restoration the same way: a longtime portrait client mentions their grandmother's photo album, the photographer offers to "take a look," and three months later they've quietly produced thirty-two restored prints for a single family's reunion. That's a profitable accident. The workflow below turns it into a service line you can quote, schedule, and invoice predictably.

You can use the [old photo restoration](/old-photo-restoration) tool in this guide as your production engine, or any of the alternatives in the comparison table — the workflow is tool-agnostic.

## Step 1 — Client Intake and Photo Pickup

A clean intake form prevents 80% of the back-and-forth that kills small studios. Capture:

- **Number of source photos** (and rough condition: pristine / faded / torn / water-damaged)
- **Final use case** (digital archive only / 8×10 reprint / canvas print / slideshow / framed gift)
- **Deadline** (and whether it's tied to an event like a memorial service or anniversary)
- **Color preference** (faithful-to-original B&W vs. colorized — see the [old photo colorization](/old-photo-colorization) workflow if the client wants color added)

Pickup options matter for trust. Mail-in is fine for digital archives, but heirloom physical prints should be hand-delivered or scanned on-site. A studio scan station with a flatbed (Epson V600 or V850) and a calibrated monitor is a one-time $700 investment that pays for itself inside four projects.

## Step 2 — Triage: Sortable, Stretchable, Surrender

After intake, sort every source photo into three buckets:

- **Sortable**: minor fading, light surface scratches, color shift. AI handles these in under a minute each.
- **Stretchable**: torn corners, water rings, missing chunks under ~15% of the image. AI plus a quick manual touchup handles these.
- **Surrender**: severe mold, missing faces, fragmentation. Be honest with the client — these should go to a conservator, not your studio.

Honest triage is the single biggest reason photographer-led restoration services keep their referral rates. If the client wants a step-by-step explanation of the triage logic, the [step-by-step restoration guide](/how-to-restore-old-photos) covers the same framework in client-facing language.

## Step 3 — Quoting: Per-Photo, Tiered, or Project Flat

Three pricing models work in the field. Pick one and stick with it for the whole project:

| Model | Per-photo rate | Best for | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Per-photo flat** | $15–$45 | Small jobs, mixed conditions | Underpricing the "stretchable" bucket |
| **Tiered (S/M/L damage)** | $10 / $25 / $60 | Mid-size collections (20–100 photos) | Tier disputes — show examples in your pricing PDF |
| **Project flat** | $400–$2,500 | Whole-album restorations, weddings | Scope creep; require a signed estimate |

For wedding photographers offering restoration as a post-wedding upsell, the project flat works best — clients are already in a "package" mindset.

## Step 4 — Scanning and Pre-Processing

If clients give you physical prints, scan at:

- **600 dpi** for 4×6 and 5×7 prints destined for digital-only delivery
- **1200 dpi** for any photo that may be reprinted larger than original
- **TIFF, not JPEG**, for everything — you'll iterate on AI passes and lossy compression compounds

Save scans into per-client folders with a strict naming convention. `2026-04-Reyes-Wedding-Album/scan-001.tiff` is dramatically easier to track than `IMG_0001.tif`.

Crop and align before AI processing. AI handles geometry corrections poorly compared to a five-second manual rotate-and-crop.

## Step 5 — Tool Selection by Damage Type

No single AI tool wins every category. The studios I work with run a small stack and route by damage type:

| Damage type | Recommended tool | Pro tip |
|---|---|---|
| Fading and color shift | ArtImageHub or Remini | ArtImageHub holds skin tone better on group portraits |
| Tears and missing chunks | ArtImageHub or Photoshop AI | Bring AI to 90% then finish in Photoshop's generative fill |
| Soft focus / motion blur | ArtImageHub photo enhancer | Test multiple passes with different denoise strengths |
| Severe water damage | Specialist conservator | Don't oversell |
| B&W → color | ArtImageHub colorizer | Always preview and let the client approve before delivery |

The full [picture restoration](/picture-restoration) tool covers fading, tears, and soft focus in a single upload — useful when you don't want to switch tools mid-batch.

## Step 6 — Batch Processing

Once your queue is sorted by damage type, run each batch through its assigned tool. Three rules:

1. **Always keep the original scan**. Save AI output to a separate folder. Never overwrite.
2. **Process in damage-type batches, not in client order**. You'll move faster running 30 fading-only photos in one tool session than jumping between tools for each photo.
3. **Watch for AI invention**. Modern AI sometimes "improves" features that weren't in the original — invented teeth in a closed-mouth smile, for instance. Compare every output to the source before delivery. The [step-by-step guide](/how-to-restore-old-photos) covers the manual QA pass in more detail.

A reasonably-priced workstation (Mac Mini M4, $599) handles 200–300 restorations per day on the workflow above.

## Step 7 — Quality Control and Client Preview

Before delivery, do a two-stage check:

- **Technical QA**: side-by-side compare against original at 100% zoom. Flag any AI-invented features.
- **Client preview**: send 3–5 sample restorations as low-res JPEG previews and ask the client to greenlight the style before you finalize the rest.

The preview step is non-negotiable for wedding and memorial work. Clients often have specific feelings about whether to keep certain scratches as "character" or remove them entirely. Asking saves a re-do.

## Step 8 — Delivery, Print, and Upsell

Final delivery options to offer:

- **Digital archive** (ZIP of full-res TIFFs) — minimum delivery for every project
- **Print package** (8×10 archival prints of top 5 restored photos) — 30–50% take rate when offered
- **Canvas or framed enlargements** — high-margin upsell, especially for wedding restoration packages

Photographers in driving range of clients can offer in-person delivery with a small print preview as part of the experience. If you serve a wider area, position your studio as remote-first — the same way the [photo restoration near you](/photo-restoration-near-me) page positions online services for clients without local options.

## Pricing Worked Example

A wedding photographer doing restoration as an album upsell:

- **Service tier**: 12-photo restoration package for the bride's parents' wedding album
- **Pricing**: $480 flat (12 × $40) — quoted upfront, included in a $1,200 anniversary album bundle
- **Studio time**: 4–6 hours (scanning + AI processing + QA + print prep)
- **Margin after material costs**: ~$380

Repeat this twice a month and you've added $9,000 in annual revenue with no new equipment, no new hires, and no new schedule pressure on the shoot side of the business.

## Common Mistakes Photographers Make

- **Skipping triage.** Don't quote until you've sorted into S/M/L. The L bucket should never be inside an AI-only workflow.
- **Single-tool dependency.** Different damage types need different models. A small stack (1 colorizer + 1 enhancer + 1 generalist restoration tool) beats any single product.
- **No client preview.** Client style preferences vary more than you'd expect on restoration. Always preview before final.
- **Charging time, not value.** Per-photo or project-flat pricing always outperforms hourly billing for this work.
