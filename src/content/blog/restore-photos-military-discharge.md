# Restoring Military Discharge and Service Record Photos

The DD-214, the Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty, has been the standard form since 1950. Before that, discharge papers took different forms — but they all served the same purpose, and many veterans saved them carefully for the rest of their lives.

These records sometimes included a photograph: the standard military portrait, the official documentation of service. Over the decades between service and death, these photographs faded in filing cabinets and strongboxes, accumulating the same damage as any other aging photograph.

When Robert's family found his grandfather's WWII discharge papers in a fireproof box after his death, they found three photographs: the official service portrait, an informal photo from training camp, and a photograph of the ship he served on. The service portrait was the family's most important find — they'd never seen a clear image of their grandfather as a young man.

## Photographs in Military Records

Military service photographs from the WWII era (1941-1945) were typically studio portraits made at military photography facilities. The quality was professional, the conditions controlled. These photographs should be in better condition than comparable civilian photographs from the same era — but "should" doesn't always hold after 80 years.

**Moisture damage** in strongboxes and filing cabinets is common. Even "fireproof" boxes are often only fire-resistant, not moisture-proof, and they can trap humidity inside.

**Fold damage** from discharge papers that were folded with the photograph inside creates crease lines through the image.

**Age-related fading** follows the same patterns as any gelatin silver print from the 1940s: silvering in the dark areas, highlight fading, overall warming of the tones.

## Restoration for Service Portraits

Military service portraits have a specific aesthetic that restoration should honor: formal, dignified, clearly photographed against a consistent backdrop. Over-processing that produces a hyper-real or stylized look is wrong for this kind of photograph.

The goal is to recover the clear, formal appearance of the original — the face of a 20-year-old in uniform, as he looked at the moment of his service.

Robert's grandfather's portrait came back clearly enough that his family could finally see the young man beneath the 80-year-old they'd known. The family had copies made for every branch of the family, and a large print made for the memorial they're planning for his centenary.
