Polaroid photos carry a particular kind of nostalgia. The instant, slightly imperfect quality of a self-developing print holds memories that feel more tactile than a digital snapshot. But the same chemistry that made Polaroid prints develop in minutes also makes them among the most fragile photographic formats to survive long-term.

Whether you have SX-70 prints from the 1970s that have turned almost entirely amber, 1990s 600-series photos with cracked surfaces, or more recent Instax prints that have picked up moisture damage, the restoration process follows the same steps — and AI has made it dramatically more accessible than it was even five years ago.

## Why Polaroid Photos Degrade Faster Than Other Prints

Traditional silver-based photographic prints, when properly stored, can survive for over a century. Polaroid prints are fundamentally different. The image is formed in a sandwich of dye layers, opacifying agents, and polymer film — all of which react to their environment over time.

UV light is the primary enemy. Even brief display near a window can shift the cyan and magenta dye layers faster than the yellow layer, causing an overall yellow-orange cast. Humidity causes the polymer layers to swell, leading to surface cracking. The plasticizers in cheap plastic storage sleeves migrate into the film and attack the image layer from the outside.

The result is predictable: most Polaroid prints over twenty years old have faded colors, yellowish casts, and some degree of surface damage. The older the print and the worse the storage conditions, the more severe the degradation.

## Scanning Your Polaroid Correctly

The quality of your scan determines how much the AI can recover. A flat, evenly-lit, high-resolution scan gives the model more pixel data to work with and produces better results.

Use a flatbed scanner if at all possible. Place the print face-down on the glass platen and set the scanner to at least 600 dpi — 1200 dpi for small-format prints or badly faded images. Save as TIFF or high-quality JPEG (quality 90 or above). Avoid photographing the print with your phone, particularly with flash, which creates reflections on the glossy surface and washes out remaining color.

Clean the scanner glass with a lint-free cloth before scanning. Dust and fingerprints on the glass show up clearly in the scan and create extra work for the restoration model.
