Hmong American family photographs document the heritage of a community whose entire American presence derives from the aftermath of the Vietnam War: Hmong people who had allied with American forces in the Secret War in Laos were in danger after the communist takeover of Laos in 1975, leading to mass refugee migration to the United States. For Hmong American families, photographs carry the particular weight of a community that faced destruction for its alliance with America.

## Cultural Heritage

Hmong American photographs document both the pre-refugee heritage that survivors maintained in their memories and the few photographs that survived the refugee experience, and the rebuilding of Hmong community life in the United States — particularly in Minnesota, California, and Wisconsin where large Hmong communities established themselves. The distinctive Hmong visual culture — the story cloth (paj ntaub) textiles that document community history in embroidered narrative, the specific traditional dress of different Hmong clans, and the New Year celebrations — appears in American photographs as evidence of cultural maintenance.

## Refugee Documentation

Hmong American photographs face specific preservation challenges related to their refugee origins: photographs that survived the escape from Laos and the years in Thai refugee camps have often experienced significant physical damage. AI restoration addresses these preservation challenges with sensitivity to the extraordinary historical circumstances of a community that came to America as a direct consequence of American foreign policy.
