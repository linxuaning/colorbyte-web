The 1940s grandparents' wedding photographs are among the most requested restoration projects I receive — the combination of historical distance (80 years), personal significance, and the specific aesthetic of 1940s formal photography creates a particularly meaningful restoration challenge.

## The 1940s Wedding Photograph Aesthetic

1940s formal wedding photography had a specific aesthetic: the strong contrast of black-and-white printing, the formal poses influenced by Hollywood portraiture, the specific fashion of the wartime and immediate postwar period. Restoration should honor this aesthetic rather than modernizing it.

## Specific Damage Patterns

1940s wedding photographs in family archives typically show 80 years of silver gelatin aging: silvering in the shadow areas, moderate to significant highlight fading, and often the acid damage from whatever album or envelope they were stored in.

## Emotional Weight

For the grandchildren who commission these restorations, the project carries unusual emotional weight — seeing a grandparent as a young person, often for the first time, in a moment of documented happiness. I approach these projects with awareness of what the restored image will mean to the family.

## Getting the Best Results

Start with the highest-quality scan you can produce — 600 DPI minimum for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small prints or photographs with faces you want to identify. Color mode scanning, even for black-and-white photographs, gives AI restoration algorithms more information to work with.

After restoration, compare the result with the original at full zoom. Check faces carefully to ensure identity is preserved, and note any areas where AI may have filled in damaged sections with plausible but uncertain reconstructions.
