## Why Family Photos Are a Special Case

General AI colorization tools are tuned on a wide mix of training images — landscapes, products, art, people. Family photos have a more specific profile: portraits dominate, lighting tends to be indoor or hazy outdoor, and the subjects often wear period-specific clothing whose colors the model has to guess.

Two practical things follow. First, models that emphasize face quality (skin tones, eye color stability, hair texture) work better than models tuned for general scenes. Second, period-specific objects (1920s flapper dress, 1940s military uniform, 1960s polyester shirt) often colorize wrong because the model picks the most statistically common color for that object shape, not the actually-likely color for that era. For historical accuracy you sometimes need to manually confirm or override the AI's choice.

## What Quality Should You Expect?

Based on synthesized framing of public test data, modern AI colorizers produce natural-looking results 70-85% of the time on typical family-photo source material. The remaining 15-30% of cases involve common failure modes:

- Skin tones that read slightly cool or grey (especially on darker skin)
- Period clothing colorized with modern color preferences
- Backgrounds (sky, foliage) that look too vivid for the era
- Black-and-white items (text, signage, jewelry) tinted unnaturally

Most users find these errors acceptable for emotional purposes (seeing grandma in color) and unacceptable for historical claims (writing "she wore a blue dress that day" based on the colorization).

## Practical Workflow for Family Archives

For a typical project of 20-100 B&W family photos, the practical workflow is:

1. **Scan or photograph all originals first** — at 600 DPI flatbed, or via Photomyne mobile if no scanner.
2. **Restore damaged ones first** — torn, badly faded, or stained photos colorize poorly. Use a restoration tool (or a bundled tool like ArtImageHub that does both) to clean before colorizing.
3. **Colorize a single representative photo** in two or three different tools. Compare. Pick the tool whose output matches what you want.
4. **Process the full archive in the chosen tool.** Keep the B&W originals filed separately — never replace them.
5. **Label colorized files clearly** (e.g., "1947_grandma_wedding_colorized.jpg") so future relatives know they are AI-generated colorizations, not the original.
