<h2>What AI Image Enhancement Actually Does</h2>

<p>Before getting to recommendations, it's worth understanding what's happening under the hood â€” because it explains why different tools excel at different tasks.</p>

<h3>Super Resolution Explained Simply</h3>

<p>When you ask an AI to upscale an image, it isn't just stretching pixels and blurring the joins the way Photoshop's Bicubic did in 2003. Modern super-resolution models (the dominant one is called Real-ESRGAN) were trained on millions of pairs of high-resolution and deliberately degraded low-resolution images. The model learned to predict what high-resolution detail <em>should</em> look like given a low-resolution input.</p>

<p>The critical word is "predict." It's generating plausible detail, not recovering real detail that was there. For most uses this is fine â€” the predicted texture looks convincing and prints beautifully. For archival or forensic purposes, it matters that what looks like a sharp brick wall was partly invented by the algorithm.</p>

<h3>Face Enhancement (GFPGAN and How It Works)</h3>

<p>Face enhancement models like GFPGAN (developed by Tencent's research team) are trained specifically on faces. They learn the structural relationships between facial features â€” how eyes, nose, and mouth relate to each other, what realistic skin texture looks like, how light falls across different facial geometries. When presented with a damaged or degraded face, the model uses these structural priors to reconstruct it.</p>

<p>This is why specialized face enhancement produces dramatically better results than general-purpose sharpening on faces. A general model treats a face like any other image region â€” looking for edges and textures to sharpen. A face model understands it's looking at a face and uses that structural knowledge to guide the reconstruction.</p>

<p>The risk: identity drift. If the original face is severely damaged, the model fills gaps based on its training distribution. It will reconstruct <em>a</em> face, not necessarily <em>the</em> face. Always compare the output to the original and flag anything that looks "not quite right" about who the person is.</p>

<h3>Noise Reduction</h3>

<p>AI noise reduction (tools like Topaz DeNoise AI) works differently from traditional methods like Luminance noise reduction in Lightroom. Traditional methods blur the noisy pixels â€” which reduces noise but also softens real detail. AI noise reduction models were trained to distinguish between noise patterns and real image structure. They suppress noise while preserving fine detail like fabric texture, individual hairs, and fine type. The difference is visible and significant for high-ISO photography.</p>

<h2>How to Get the Best Results from AI Enhancement</h2>

<p>Even the best AI enhancer is only as good as what you give it. Here's what I've learned after testing forty photos across seven tools:</p>

<h3>Start with the Cleanest Version You Have</h3>

<p>For old photos, this means scanning them yourself rather than photographing them on a phone. A flat-bed scanner at 600 DPI will give you far more information to work with than a phone camera shot of the same print â€” and more information means better AI output. Use TIFF or PNG format, not JPEG, which introduces compression artifacts that confuse damage-detection algorithms.</p>

<p>For digital photos, find the original file, not a social-media compressed version. That Instagram download has been compressed to maybe 15% of the original file size. Start with the phone's original HEIC or JPG file from the camera roll.</p>

<h3>Don't Over-Enhance</h3>

<p>Every AI tool has a slider or setting for enhancement strength, and the temptation is always to push it toward maximum. Resist this. Over-enhanced photos look artificial: faces go plastic, textures look painted, and the "AI look" becomes obvious. I default to 70â€“80% strength on face enhancement settings and only push higher if the original is severely damaged.</p>

<p>There's also a subtler problem: the more aggressively you enhance, the more the AI reconstructs rather than restores. At moderate settings, you're recovering what was there. At maximum settings, you're getting the AI's idea of what should have been there. For photos of real people, the distinction matters.</p>

<h3>When to Stop</h3>

<p>Compare the enhanced output to the original before deciding you're done. Check three things: (1) Are faces still recognizable as the same person? (2) Are there new details in the background that weren't in the original? (3) Does the overall tone and character of the photo feel right?</p>

<p>Sometimes the AI over-corrects on color â€” making a naturally warm old photo look cooler and more "modern." Sometimes it's over-eager about perceived scratches and softens real fine detail. When you notice these things, try downloading the result and doing a quick manual level correction in any basic editor, or try a lower enhancement strength. The AI is a powerful starting point, not the final word.</p>

<p>And if a photo is genuinely irreplaceable â€” the only image of a person who's gone â€” consider using AI as a first pass and then spending a hundred dollars to have a professional restoration artist review and touch up the result. AI gets you 85â€“90% of the way there. For that last 10â€“15% on a photo that matters most, the human touch is worth the cost.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<p><strong>Can AI enhance a photo that's extremely blurry or tiny?</strong><br>
<strong>Short answer:</strong> It depends on how much information survives. Small but sharp originals respond well to AI upscaling. Blurry originals are harder â€” AI can sharpen apparent detail but can't recover information that was never captured.</p>

<p>The practical threshold: if the main subject (especially a face) is at least 128Ã—128 pixels in the original, modern face enhancement models have enough to work with. Below that, results become unpredictable. For old tiny prints (wallet photos, photo booth strips), scan at 1200 DPI to give the AI more pixels to analyze before enhancement.</p>

<p><strong>Will AI enhancement change how my relatives look in old photos?</strong><br>
<strong>Short answer:</strong> Modern tools are designed to preserve identity, but it's imperfect. Always compare the output to the original and flag anything that looks "not quite right" about the person.</p>

<p>Face restoration models include identity preservation in their training objectives â€” meaning they're penalized during training for changing who someone looks like. In practice, results are accurate 90â€“95% of the time. The failure cases typically involve severely damaged photos where the AI has to reconstruct from very little. If the output face doesn't look right to you, trust your instinct â€” you know what your relative looked like better than the algorithm does.</p>

<p><strong>Is there a difference between "AI upscaling" and "AI enhancement"?</strong><br>
<strong>Short answer:</strong> Yes. Upscaling makes an image bigger. Enhancement improves image quality by repairing damage, reducing noise, sharpening, or correcting color. The best tools do both, but they're different processes.</p>

<p>You can upscale without enhancing (Let's Enhance on a clean small photo) and you can enhance without upscaling (noise reduction on a large RAW file). For old scanned photos, you typically want both â€” enhancement to repair damage and correct fading, then upscaling to produce a print-ready file. Tools like ArtImageHub do this in a single pipeline.</p>
