You come home to a flooded basement. Or a burst pipe upstairs has soaked the closet where you stored family albums. Or a hurricane swept water through your home. Decades of family photographs are wet, stuck together, smelling of mildew. The clock is now running.

This guide walks through the urgent emergency response and the longer restoration workflow for water-damaged photo collections.

## The 48-hour critical window

Water-damaged photos deteriorate quickly:
- **0-24 hours**: Photos can typically be saved with standard drying
- **24-48 hours**: Mold begins to develop; emulsion may start to soften
- **48-72 hours**: Stuck-together photos may fuse permanently; significant mold growth
- **72+ hours**: Many photos are unrecoverable without professional intervention

If you can't act within 48 hours, **freeze the wet photos in sealed plastic bags**. Freezing arrests deterioration. You can then thaw and process small batches over weeks or months.

## Step 1: Emergency drying

Equipment you need:
- Clean cotton towels or paper towels
- Flat absorbent surfaces (cardboard, blotting paper, even bath towels)
- Fans for air circulation (NOT direct heat)
- Dehumidifier if available
- Latex or cotton gloves to handle wet prints

Process:
1. Gently rinse photos in clean cool water if covered in dirt or debris (avoid scrubbing)
2. Lay each photo face-up on absorbent surface
3. Don't stack photos until completely dry
4. Run fans for air circulation
5. If photos are stuck together, soak the stuck pile in clean cool water for 30-60 minutes; many will separate on their own
6. Photos typically dry in 24-48 hours under good conditions

**Important**: Don't use direct heat (hair dryers, ovens, heaters). Heat warps prints and can melt emulsion. Air circulation at room temperature is correct.

## Step 2: Stuck-together print rescue

For photos that won't separate after soaking:
- **Don't force them apart** — emulsion will tear permanently
- Place stuck pile in sealed plastic bag with damp paper towels
- Keep refrigerated until you can take them to a professional photo conservator
- Conservators have humidity-chamber techniques to safely separate stuck prints

If professional conservation isn't accessible, freezing the stuck prints arrests deterioration indefinitely.

## Step 3: Triage and digital backup

Once dried, sort photos by significance:
- Irreplaceable family photos (only-existing portraits)
- Important but reproducible photos (have copies elsewhere)
- Generic or low-significance photos

For all priority levels, **scan at 600+ DPI** as soon as photos are stable enough to handle. Digital scans are insurance against any future deterioration. The scanning order should follow priority — protect the irreplaceable photos first.

Save scans as TIFF or PNG (uncompressed) for the master files.

## Step 4: Assessing post-drying damage

Even successfully-dried water-damaged photos typically show:
- **Tide marks**: Brown lines where the water level reached
- **Color streaks**: Especially on dyed-image color prints (1970s-1990s)
- **Warping or curling**: Paper that doesn't lie flat
- **Surface haziness**: Fine white deposits from dissolved minerals
- **Emulsion damage**: In severe cases, areas where the image surface has lifted or peeled

Triage which damage AI can address (tide marks, color streaks) versus which needs manual or professional intervention (missing emulsion, paper structural failure).

## Step 5: Manual cleanup for severe damage

For photos with missing emulsion areas (where the image is gone, not just damaged):
1. Open the scan in Photoshop or similar
2. Use content-aware fill or clone stamp to reconstruct missing areas
3. Save the manually-cleaned version
4. THEN run AI restoration on the cleaned version

Skipping the manual cleanup step results in AI hallucinating content for missing areas, which is usually wrong in noticeable ways.

## Step 7: Document and archive

For flood-damaged collections, careful metadata matters:
- Date of water-damage event (e.g., "Hurricane Henri, 2021-08-22")
- Drying method used (e.g., "Air-dried 48 hours, no heat")
- Restoration date and tool (e.g., "AI-restored with ArtImageHub, 2026-05")
- Any manual cleanup performed (e.g., "Content-aware fill on missing corner")

This documentation helps future researchers understand what they're seeing.

## Special case: insurance documentation

If the water damage was from a covered event (flood, burst pipe, hurricane), insurance may compensate for restoration costs. Before discarding any damaged photos:
- Photograph the damage extent (overall album state, individual prints with visible damage)
- Document each significant photo's pre-restoration condition
- Save receipts from any professional conservation services
- Maintain the archive of damaged-vs-restored versions for claim documentation

## Realistic expectations

Severely water-damaged photos can be improved but not fully restored. Some emulsion damage is permanent. Some color shifts cannot be fully reversed. The realistic goal is preserving family-history value, not creating museum-perfect reproductions.

For irreplaceable photos with severe damage, consider that "imperfect restoration" still preserves more memory than "no restoration". A faded photo with visible water damage but recognizable faces is more valuable than a destroyed photo.

## Final note

Water-damaged photo collections are emergencies that demand fast action and careful triage. The 48-hour window is real. Within it, even significant damage is recoverable through emergency drying + scanning + AI restoration. Beyond it, freezing and professional conservation may still rescue treasured family memories.
