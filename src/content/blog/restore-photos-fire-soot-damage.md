Photographs that survive a house fire often do so in very specific ways: completely sealed in albums or metal boxes may emerge surprisingly intact despite the surrounding destruction; photographs in open storage often show heat curling, soot deposition, and smoke damage; photographs close to the fire's origin may show partial burning. Working with fire-damaged photographs requires understanding these different survival scenarios and applying appropriate physical and digital restoration techniques. Once scanned, AI can [restore damaged photos](/restore-damaged-photos) — fixing soot marks, heat discoloration, and partial burns digitally.

## What Saves Photos in a Fire

Photographs survive fires through combinations of factors: physical protection from album covers and boxes that char on the outside while protecting contents; moisture from firefighting water that counterintuitively helps by keeping temperature below photographic damage thresholds in some conditions; and simple distance from the fire's source. Metal boxes and fireproof safes designed for document storage can protect photographs at temperatures that would destroy unprotected materials. Understanding what protected your photographs helps assess what physical treatment they may need before digitizing.

## Physical Treatment for Soot-Covered Photographs

Photographs covered with fire soot or char should be handled with care before any physical cleaning. Soot is composed of fine carbon particles and various combustion products, some of which may be chemically harmful. Work outdoors or in well-ventilated spaces when handling soot-covered photographs. Loose, powdery soot can sometimes be gently lifted with a soft brush in long strokes that move the soot off the image rather than grinding it in. Do not use water on soot-covered photographs — water converts powdery soot into a paste that penetrates the emulsion. Consultation with a professional conservator is advisable for severely soot-damaged photographs.

## Digital Restoration After Physical Treatment

After physical treatment and high-resolution scanning, AI restoration addresses the residual damage from fire exposure: the discoloration from soot deposits, the color shift from heat and smoke exposure, surface texture changes from the physical effects of high temperature, and in some cases partial charring that obscures image detail. The most challenging aspect of fire restoration is areas where the physical photograph was partially destroyed — the emulsion layer melted, the paper support charred, or the image material was chemically altered by extreme heat. In these areas, AI can provide plausible reconstruction based on surrounding surviving content, but accuracy cannot be guaranteed.
