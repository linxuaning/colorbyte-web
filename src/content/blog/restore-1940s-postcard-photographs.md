# Restoring 1940s Real Photo Postcards

Before the era of mass-produced photo printing, there was a photographic format that combined the functionality of a postcard with the quality of a photograph: the Real Photo Postcard, or RPPC. These were actual silver gelatin photographs printed on postcard-weight paper with a preprinted postcard back.

RPPCs were made from approximately 1900 through the 1940s, with peak production in the 1900s-1920s. They documented everything: main streets in small towns, disaster scenes, events, family portraits, unusual subjects that photographers thought might sell. Many families have them in collections without knowing what they are.

## Identifying a Real Photo Postcard

RPPCs are distinguished from printed postcards by examining the image under magnification. A printed postcard shows a dot pattern (halftone printing). A RPPC shows the continuous tone of a photographic print — no dots, just varying densities of silver.

The postcard back typically shows a preprinted design with stamp box and address area. Certain backs have date codes that allow precise dating: Kodak, Ansco, and other manufacturers coded their postcard paper.

## Typical RPPC Damage

The silver gelatin surface of RPPCs shows the same age-related changes as other silver gelatin prints: silver mirroring, highlight fading, and yellowing. The thinner postcard paper is more prone to physical damage — creasing, tearing, and warping from moisture.

**Writing on the front:** Some RPPCs have handwritten captions or annotations on the image side — either in the image margin or directly on the photograph. This writing is historical documentation that restoration should preserve, not remove.

**Postmarks:** The stamp side sometimes preserves a postmark that provides a specific date for when the card was mailed. Photograph the back before restoration work.

## Restoration Approach

RPPCs respond well to standard silver gelatin restoration approaches: tonal correction, face enhancement, inpainting for physical damage.

The small format (approximately 3.5 × 5.5 inches) means scanning at high resolution (1200 DPI) is important for preserving the detail that RPPCs captured.

For postcards showing buildings, streets, and community scenes — historically significant content — the AI's handling of architectural and environmental detail is particularly important. These images often document places that no longer exist.
