## Why decade matters for restoration

Photo restoration calibration changes by era:

### Pre-1900: Silver-based unique processes

[Daguerreotypes](/blog/restore-daguerreotype-images) (1839-1860s) and [tintypes](/blog/restore-old-tintypes) require special handling — never touch the silvered surface, never use water. Scan through protective glass at oblique angle. AI restoration via Real-ESRGAN dramatically improves the digital scan.

[Carte de visite](/blog/restore-carte-de-visite-photos) and [cabinet cards](/blog/restore-victorian-cabinet-card) (1860s-1900s) are albumen prints on cardstock. Scan front + back at 1200+ DPI; photographer imprints on backs hold genealogical metadata.

### 1900s-1910s: Silver gelatin era

[1900s portraits](/blog/restore-1900s-portraits-complete-guide) typically suffer silver mirroring — visible as metallic sheen on dark areas. AI restoration via GFPGAN recovers face detail; Real-ESRGAN sharpens edges. WWI service photos ([1910s WWI guide](/blog/restore-1910s-world-war-one-photos)) face the same issues plus added age (105-110 years).

### 1920s-1940s: Snapshot era + WWII

The 1920s [Jazz Age](/blog/restore-1920s-flapper-photos), [Prohibition era](/blog/restore-1920s-prohibition-era-photos), and [aviation pioneer](/blog/restore-1920s-aviation-pioneer-photos) photos transitioned from formal studio to candid hand-held snapshots. Smaller format means higher-DPI scanning is essential.

The 1930s [Depression era](/blog/restore-1930s-depression-era-photos) and [Dust Bowl photos](/blog/restore-1930s-dust-bowl-photo-prints) provide era reference via the [Library of Congress FSA collection](https://www.loc.gov/collections/fsa-owi-black-and-white-negatives/) for color calibration.

The 1940s [WWII era](/blog/restore-wwii-soldier-photos) photos approaching their 80th-85th anniversaries are receiving renewed family attention, with both [home front photos](/blog/restore-1940s-home-front-photos) and [wartime weddings](/blog/restore-1940s-wedding-photos-wartime) commonly being digitized.

### 1950s-1970s: Color era begins

[1950s color photos](/blog/restore-1950s-color-photos) introduced Kodachrome, which fades to magenta over decades. [1950s suburban family photos](/blog/restore-1950s-suburban-family-photos) and [Kodak prints](/blog/restore-1950s-kodak-prints) all follow similar restoration patterns.

The 1960s saw [civil rights documentation](/blog/restore-1960s-civil-rights-era-photos), [counterculture](/blog/restore-1960s-counterculture-photos), and [space age](/blog/restore-1960s-space-age-photos) photographs. [Color slide projector photos](/blog/restore-1960s-slide-projector-photos) need slide-specific handling.

The 1970s introduced [Polaroid SX-70](/blog/restore-1970s-polaroid-sx70-photos) instant photography with unique tonal degradation, plus [Instamatic prints](/blog/restore-1970s-instamatic-photos), [disco era](/blog/restore-1970s-disco-photos), and [school yearbook](/blog/restore-1970s-school-yearbook-photos) photos.

### 1980s-2000s: Color print era + magnetic albums

The 1980s introduced the [magnetic photo album](/blog/remove-glue-residue-album-photos) — the worst photo storage format ever sold. PVC adhesive yellows, off-gasses, and bonds prints irreversibly. Move ALL family photos out of magnetic albums to archival sleeves; this is the highest-priority preservation action for most US families.

[1980s polaroid family photos](/blog/restore-1980s-polaroid-family-photos), [neon photos](/blog/restore-1980s-neon-photos), and [birthday party photos](/blog/restore-1980s-birthday-party-photos) all share C-print color shift challenges.
