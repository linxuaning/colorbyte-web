Somewhere in most family collections there exists a formal wedding portrait of a great-grandparent or great-great-grandparent — a stiff, posed studio photograph from the late 1800s or early 1900s that captures the formal solemnity of matrimony in an era when photographs were rare and precious. These portraits have often survived 100+ years but show every decade of their age in cracks, foxing, silver mirroring, and overall loss of contrast. Restoring them connects living family members to ancestors they may never have met.

## Characteristics of Victorian and Edwardian Wedding Portraits

Formal wedding portraits from 1880–1915 have distinctive characteristics that reflect the photography technology and social customs of the era. Exposures required subjects to remain still for several seconds, producing the characteristic unsmiling, rigid poses. Studio backdrops were typically elaborate painted scenes or simple curtain arrangements. Brides wore high-necked dresses in cream, white, or even black (a fashionable choice in some eras). These images were often printed as cabinet cards (4.25×6.5 inches) or larger albumens, sometimes with elaborate decorative mounts.

## Common Damage Patterns in Century-Old Portraits

The most common damage in photos from this era includes silver mirroring (a silvery sheen over dark areas caused by migration of silver to the photo surface), foxing (reddish-brown spots from oxidation and mold), overall fading due to chemical breakdown, and physical damage from handling — creases, tears, and corner rounding. Photos that were kept in albums often show adhesive damage on the back and pressure marks from album pages pressing against them. Cards may have handwritten notations on the back faded to near-invisibility.
