# How to Digitize Old Photos in 2026: The Complete Guide (Scanner vs Phone vs Service)

My mother called me on a Sunday afternoon in October to tell me they were selling the house. My parents had lived in that house for 38 years. I drove over that evening, and while my mother made tea and my father watched football in the other room, I started going through the hall closet where, behind the winter coats and the box of mystery cables and the baby shoes she'd never thrown away, I found it.

A cardboard box. One of those banker's boxes that could have contained files from an accountant's office. Except when I opened it, it was full of photographs. Loose photographs, not in albums â€” hundreds of them, maybe three hundred, covering forty years of my parents' lives and my childhood and things that happened before I was born. My grandfather in his twenties, looking like a stranger. My parents' first apartment. Me at five with a haircut I don't remember and an expression I'd never seen on my own face.

The box smelled faintly of something I couldn't identify. I pulled out one of the photographs from the 1970s and it had that faint, slightly chemical smell that I've learned since is the early stage of color dye instability. The cyan layer fading. The clock quietly ticking.

I put the box in my car that night. Over the next three weeks, I scanned every photograph in it. And I've been helping other families do the same thing â€” and avoid the mistakes I made that first time â€” ever since.

Here is everything I know about digitizing old photographs.

<h2>Why Digitizing Urgently Matters</h2>

<p>I want to explain why I use the word "urgently" without being alarmist, because I've seen both ends of this â€” families who acted in time and families who didn't.</p>

<h3>What's Actually Happening to Your Prints Right Now</h3>

<p>Photographic prints are not stable objects. They are the end product of a chemical reaction, and like all chemical reactions, they don't stop â€” they continue until the materials are depleted or destroyed. The specific degradation pathway depends on the print type:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Black-and-white silver gelatin prints (pre-1970s mostly):</strong> Silver particles migrate and oxidize, creating silver mirroring â€” that metallic sheen you see on the edges and highlights. The image fades as silver density decreases. In typical home storage conditions, noticeable fading begins within 50â€“100 years, though poor storage (high humidity, temperature fluctuations) accelerates this dramatically.</li>
  <li><strong>Color prints from the 1970sâ€“1990s:</strong> The three dye layers (cyan, magenta, yellow) fade at different rates, with cyan typically fading fastest. The result is the orange-red cast you see on most photos from this era. These prints can show significant color shift within 30â€“40 years of storage. Kodacolor and Fujicolor prints from the 1980s are often already partially shifted even in well-maintained albums.</li>
  <li><strong>Polaroids and instant prints:</strong> Notoriously unstable. Some Polaroid formats are already significantly faded within 20â€“25 years. If you have Polaroids, these are your most time-sensitive items.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Silent Disasters: Vinegar Syndrome, Mold, and Disasters</h3>

<p>Beyond gradual chemical fading, there are acute threats. <strong>Vinegar syndrome</strong> â€” the breakdown of acetate film bases â€” releases acetic acid that accelerates the decomposition of anything nearby. If you open a box and smell vinegar, you have a problem that is actively getting worse. This affects film more than prints, but if negatives and prints are stored together, the negatives can contaminate the prints.</p>

<p>Mold thrives in humid storage conditions and can render photographs permanently unrecoverable within weeks of an outbreak. Water damage from flooding, burst pipes, or roof leaks can destroy a lifetime of photographs in hours. Fire is obvious. I work with families after all of these events, and the thing that makes the outcome tolerable â€” or sometimes even good â€” is when some of the collection had already been digitized.</p>

<p>A family I worked with in Seattle lost their basement storage room to a burst pipe two winters ago. Everything on the bottom two shelves was destroyed: Christmas ornaments, tax records, boxes of books â€” and one box of photographs. But because they'd attended one of my digitization workshops the previous spring and scanned about 60% of their collection, those 60% survived in digital form. The physical prints were gone, but the images weren't.</p>

<h2>Method 1: Flatbed Scanner (Best Quality)</h2>

<p>If you want the best possible digital capture of your photographs â€” and for irreplaceable prints, you do â€” a flatbed scanner is the right tool. There is no phone app, no camera rig, and no service that routinely produces better scans of individual prints than a quality flatbed scanner operated correctly.</p>

<h3>What Resolution to Use</h3>

<p>Resolution is the most important scanning decision you'll make, and it's also the one that most people get wrong. Too low and you've captured a permanently limited file; you can't add resolution later. Too high and you create unmanageably large files for no practical benefit.</p>

<p>My recommendations:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Standard 4Ã—6 prints: 600 DPI.</strong> This produces a file roughly 3600Ã—2400 pixels â€” more than enough to print at 12Ã—8 or larger, and well above what AI restoration tools need to work with.</li>
  <li><strong>3Ã—5 prints: 600â€“800 DPI.</strong> The smaller the original, the higher the DPI needed to capture equivalent detail.</li>
  <li><strong>Wallet-size (approximately 2.5Ã—3.5): 1200 DPI.</strong> These are small originals with fine detail â€” school portraits, formal ID-style photos â€” that need higher resolution to capture properly.</li>
  <li><strong>2Ã—2 and smaller: 1200â€“2400 DPI.</strong> Very small prints deserve very high resolution. At 2400 DPI, a 2Ã—2 print produces a 4800Ã—4800 pixel file â€” workable for large-format printing after AI upscaling.</li>
</ul>

<p>One thing I tell everyone at my workshops: <strong>scan your most important photos at double the resolution you think you need.</strong> Storage is cheap. You can't go back and rescan a photo that's deteriorated further by the time you realize you wanted more resolution.</p>

<h3>Which Scanner to Buy</h3>

<p>For home use, the <strong>Epson Perfection series</strong> is the standard recommendation and for good reason. The V39 (roughly $90) handles standard prints well at up to 4800 DPI. The V600 (~$230) adds film and slide scanning capability, which matters if you have negatives or slides to digitize as well. Both are reliable, produce accurate color, and have been the workhorses of home digitization projects for over a decade.</p>

<p>If you're processing a very large collection â€” hundreds of photos â€” consider the Epson FastFoto FF-680W, which is an auto-feed scanner designed specifically for photographs. It can scan at roughly one photo every two seconds with good quality. The tradeoff: auto-feed is harder on fragile prints, and very old or brittle photos should always be manually placed on a flatbed. But for a box of 500 reasonably well-preserved prints from the 1980s and 1990s, it's a practical time-saver.</p>

<h3>The Workflow: Clean First, Scan Correctly, Save Right</h3>

<p>These three steps are where most people skip corners, and the skipped corners show up in the results.</p>

<p><strong>Clean first.</strong> Dust and fingerprints scanned at 600 DPI look enormous and can be mistaken for image damage. Use a soft anti-static brush (the kind sold for camera sensors works perfectly) to lightly brush the surface of each print before scanning. For fingerprints, a very lightly dampened microfiber cloth â€” barely damp, not wet â€” held against the print briefly and then buffed dry will usually remove fresh prints. Do not use cleaning solutions on old prints. For very old prints with surface sensitivity, dust only; any moisture is a risk.</p>

<p><strong>Scan correctly.</strong> Place the print face-down on the scanner glass. Verify it's aligned straight â€” crooked scans can be straightened in software but it's better to get it right at capture. Close the scanner lid fully, or if the print is thick (mounted photos, some daguerreotype cases), use a black cloth or dark cardboard over the open scanner to reduce ambient light interference. Preview the scan, confirm the crop, then scan at your target resolution.</p>

<p><strong>Save as TIFF.</strong> This is the single most debated point in digitization workflows and I'll be direct: save your original scans as TIFF files. TIFF is lossless â€” no compression artifacts, no generational quality loss if you re-save. Yes, the files are large. A 600 DPI scan of a 4Ã—6 print as TIFF is roughly 60â€“90 MB. That sounds alarming until you realize that a 4TB external drive costs about $80 and holds roughly 40,000â€“60,000 such files. The question isn't whether you can afford the storage. The question is whether you can afford to redo your entire digitization project in ten years because the JPEG quality was set too low.</p>

<p>Save JPEGs too â€” smaller file sizes are useful for sharing and online storage. But keep the TIFFs as your archival masters. Always.</p>

<h2>Method 2: Smartphone Scanner Apps</h2>

<p>I want to give you an honest assessment here, because there's a lot of enthusiastic marketing around phone scanning apps and the reality is more complicated.</p>

<h3>Google PhotoScan and Microsoft Lens</h3>

<p><strong>Google PhotoScan</strong> solves a genuine problem with phone photography of prints: glare. It captures four overlapping images with the phone at slightly different positions, then algorithmically removes the reflection pattern. For prints with surface glare â€” glossy prints, laminated photos â€” this makes a real difference. The result is better than a single phone photo, though the multi-image stitching can occasionally produce slight blurring at the stitching boundaries.</p>

<p><strong>Microsoft Lens</strong> is primarily a document scanner app but handles photographs adequately. It provides perspective correction (useful if you can't position the phone perfectly parallel to the print) and automatic cropping. The image quality is limited by your phone's camera, not by the app.</p>

<h3>Honest Limitations</h3>

<p>Phone scanning apps will not match a flatbed scanner for quality. Full stop. The limitations are physical, not software:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Depth of field:</strong> Phone lenses have a focal depth range. Prints that aren't perfectly flat â€” curled edges, mounted photos with slight warping â€” will have soft areas at the edges. A flatbed scanner places the sensor directly above the print at a fixed, optimized distance.</li>
  <li><strong>Lighting:</strong> Even with glare-removal algorithms, phone scanning captures ambient light that can create uneven illumination across the print. Scanner glass with integrated LED lighting produces uniform, controlled illumination every time.</li>
  <li><strong>Pixel density:</strong> The best smartphone cameras capture roughly 12â€“50 megapixels. A 600 DPI scan of a 4Ã—6 print is approximately 8.6 megapixels. On paper, a good phone "wins" on pixel count â€” but the scanner captures those pixels with more consistent quality because the capture geometry is controlled.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When Phone Scanning Is Acceptable</h3>

<p>I don't want to be a purist about this. Phone scanning is genuinely better than not scanning at all, and there are contexts where it makes sense:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Large volumes where good-enough is sufficient.</strong> If your goal is to share a collection of 400 prints with family members who will view them on phone screens, phone scanning gets the job done faster than flatbed scanning and the quality difference won't matter for that use case.</li>
  <li><strong>Inaccessible originals.</strong> If you're visiting a relative and they have prints you want to capture but you can't take them home to scan, a phone photo is infinitely better than nothing.</li>
  <li><strong>Quick identification sweeps.</strong> Before beginning a scanning project, I often photograph the entire contents of a box quickly to create a low-quality inventory. This lets me prioritize and plan before starting the careful scanning work.</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not use phone scanning as your only capture method for your most irreplaceable photographs. For those, use a flatbed scanner. If you don't have access to one, many libraries, FedEx Office locations, and photography stores offer per-use flatbed scanning, typically for $0.25â€“$1.00 per photo.</p>

<h2>Method 3: Professional Scanning Services</h2>

<p>For families who've inherited a large collection â€” the proverbial box of 300 photographs from a parent's estate â€” professional scanning services are a legitimate option that many people don't consider.</p>

<h3>What It Costs</h3>

<p>Pricing varies significantly:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Budget postal services</strong> (ScanMyPhotos, Legacybox): $0.08â€“$0.20 per standard print. You mail in your photos, they return them with a USB drive or digital download. The quality is serviceable â€” typically 600 DPI JPEG â€” but these services process volume, not individual attention.</li>
  <li><strong>Mid-range services:</strong> $0.20â€“$0.35 per print, with options for higher resolution and TIFF output.</li>
  <li><strong>High-end archival services:</strong> $0.40â€“$1.00+ per print, with dust removal, color-managed workflows, and detailed handling of fragile originals. Worth it for museums, institutions, and the most irreplaceable family items.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Postal vs Local Services</h3>

<p>Postal services are less expensive and convenient for large volumes. The main concern people raise is risk â€” mailing irreplaceable originals. Legitimate services use trackable shipping and professional handling, and losses are rare. But for genuinely irreplaceable items (a single surviving portrait of a deceased parent, for instance), I'd recommend local scanning or doing it yourself rather than mailing it away.</p>

<p>Local services through photography studios, archival conservators, or university library digitization labs offer more accountability and usually better handling for delicate originals. Ask specifically whether they do dust removal before scanning and whether they offer TIFF output.</p>

<h3>When Professional Services Make Sense</h3>

<p>The honest answer: when you've inherited a large box and the time cost of scanning it yourself is the real barrier to action. I've worked with families who had 500+ photos sitting undigitized for five years because "I'll get to it eventually" â€” and didn't. A professional service at $150 for 500 photos removed the time barrier and got the digitization done. Imperfect digitization that happens is better than perfect digitization that doesn't.</p>

<h2>After Digitizing: The Next Step</h2>

<p>You've scanned 300 photos. Now what? Most people stop at this point, which means they've done the hard work of capture but haven't completed the preservation workflow. Here's what comes next.</p>

<h3>Organize with a Naming Convention</h3>

<p>Don't leave your scans as IMG_4521.jpg. You will have no idea what that file contains in ten years. My recommended naming convention: <code>YYYY-MM_Description_NNN.tif</code></p>

<p>For example: <code>1978-07_Family-Vacation-Maine_001.tif</code>, <code>1978-07_Family-Vacation-Maine_002.tif</code>.</p>

<p>For undated photos, estimate the decade: <code>1960s-approx_Grandma-Wedding-Portrait_001.tif</code></p>

<p>If you have metadata about the photograph â€” names, dates, locations, occasions â€” add it to the file's EXIF metadata using a free tool like ExifTool or digiKam. This information will survive indefinitely as long as the file does, and it means future family members will know who is in the photograph without having to ask anyone.</p>

<h3>Back Up in Three Places: The 3-2-1 Rule</h3>

<p>This is the non-negotiable rule of digital preservation. The 3-2-1 rule: <strong>3 copies, on 2 different media types, with 1 off-site.</strong></p>

<p>In practice for most families: original files on your computer's hard drive (copy 1), a backup external hard drive at home (copy 2), and cloud storage (copy 3, off-site). Google Photos, Amazon Photos, and Backblaze all offer reasonably priced cloud backup for large photo libraries. I use Backblaze for archival files because it supports TIFF and doesn't compress on upload, and Google Photos as an accessible copy for sharing.</p>

<p>Check your backups periodically â€” at minimum once a year. External hard drives fail. Cloud services change their terms. The 3-2-1 rule only works if all three copies are actually up to date.</p>

<h2>Common Digitization Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

<p>In seven years of running digitization workshops, I've seen the same mistakes repeatedly. Here are the ones that matter most.</p>

<h3>Scanning at Too Low a Resolution</h3>

<p>This is the irreversible mistake. You can't add resolution to an existing scan â€” you can only rescan the original. I've had families contact me asking if something could be done to "sharpen" their scans, and when I see 150 DPI files from 4Ã—6 prints, the answer is: not meaningfully. The AI can upscale, but upscaling a 150 DPI scan produces a larger blurry image, not a larger sharp one.</p>

<p>If you're in doubt, scan higher. 600 DPI for standard prints is my minimum recommendation, not my target. I personally scan everything at 600 DPI and my most important photos at 1200 DPI.</p>

<h3>Saving Originals as JPEG Instead of TIFF</h3>

<p>JPEG is a lossy format â€” it discards information to reduce file size. Every time you save a JPEG, you lose a little more information. This is called generational loss, and it accumulates. Your archival master should be TIFF. Save JPEG copies for sharing and viewing, but treat the TIFF as the document of record. JPEG compression artifacts also confuse AI restoration tools, which can misidentify compression artifacts as physical damage â€” an avoidable problem if you're working from TIFF originals.</p>

<h3>Not Cleaning Prints Before Scanning</h3>

<p>Dust and lint are the enemy of high-resolution scanning. At 600 DPI, a small piece of dust shows up as a clearly visible dark spot. A fingerprint becomes a prominent smear. Worse, if you're going to run AI restoration on the scans, the tool may attempt to "fix" the dust spot as if it were image damage, potentially creating a visible artifact in the correction area.</p>

<p>Two minutes with an anti-static brush before scanning each print eliminates 90% of this problem. It's the highest-return habit you can develop for a digitization project.</p>

<h3>Not Scanning Both Sides of Prints</h3>

<p>The back of old photographs is often a primary source of information: handwritten names, dates, locations, event descriptions, photographer studio names and addresses that can help date the image. Scan the back of every photograph that has any writing on it. File the reverse scan alongside the front scan with a "_back" suffix in the filename. I've had families discover the names of people they'd never been able to identify, simply because we scanned the backs of unlabeled portraits that someone had written on decades ago.</p>

<h3>Doing Everything at Once (and Burning Out)</h3>

<p>I've watched this kill projects. A family opens a box of 400 photos with great intentions, scans 50 over a weekend, and then doesn't touch it again for three years. The goal should be sustainable progress, not a single heroic effort.</p>

<p>My recommended approach: prioritize before you start. Sort photos into three groups â€” irreplaceable (scan these first, at highest quality), important (scan second), and nice-to-have (scan last, or outsource to a service). Scan irreplaceable photos in your first session. If the project stalls after that, at least the most important photographs are preserved.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>What is the best resolution to scan old photos for restoration?</h3>
<p>600 DPI for standard 4Ã—6 and 5Ã—7 prints; 1200 DPI for wallet-size and smaller prints; 1200 DPI for any print you plan to enlarge significantly. The resolution of your scan determines the ceiling for what AI restoration can achieve â€” starting from a 600 DPI scan, AI upscaling can produce a file suitable for 16Ã—20 or larger prints. Starting from a 150 DPI scan, AI upscaling produces a larger but still soft result. Save as TIFF for archival masters. If you're scanning specifically for AI restoration via a tool like <a href="/old-photo-restoration">ArtImageHub</a>, 600 DPI is the practical minimum for the face restoration algorithms to work well â€” below that, faces are often too small in the scan for the model to detect and restore properly.</p>

<h3>Is it worth paying for a professional scanning service?</h3>
<p>For collections under about 100 photos: do it yourself with a flatbed scanner. The per-photo time investment is manageable, and you maintain control over quality settings and file formats. For collections of 200â€“500 photos where the time cost is the barrier to action: professional services are worth considering, especially budget postal services at $0.08â€“$0.20 per photo. For collections over 500 photos, or any collection that includes film negatives and slides as well as prints: professional services or the Epson FastFoto auto-feed scanner become genuinely practical choices. The most expensive mistake is leaving a large collection undigitized indefinitely â€” imperfect digitization that happens beats perfect digitization that doesn't.</p>

<h3>Can I use my iPhone to scan old photos?</h3>
<p>Yes, with caveats. iPhone cameras (and high-end Android cameras) can produce usable scans of photographs, especially with apps like Google PhotoScan that address the glare problem. The quality won't match a flatbed scanner â€” particularly for small prints, where the physical sensor-to-subject distance creates lens limitations â€” but for capturing a photo that exists only in one location, a good phone photo is far better than nothing. If you use your phone to scan, use the stock camera app rather than a document scanner app, shoot in good indirect natural light, hold the phone directly parallel to the print, and use the highest quality setting available. For your most important prints, find a flatbed scanner. For casual preservation or when a scanner isn't available, your phone is a legitimate tool.</p>

<h3>How do I organize thousands of digitized photos?</h3>
<p>Start with chronological folder organization: a top-level folder per decade, subfolders per year or event. Apply a consistent file naming convention immediately on import â€” not later, because "later" rarely comes. Include at minimum the approximate date and a brief description in the filename. For family identification metadata (who's in the photo), digiKam and Apple Photos both support face tagging that can help you organize a large collection over time. The most important principle: organize as you scan, not after. It takes maybe 15 extra seconds per photo to give it a useful filename while you still know the context. Doing it later, when you're staring at 400 files called "scan001.jpg," is an entirely different and much more discouraging project.</p>
