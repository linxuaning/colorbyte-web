The most famous railway photograph in American history is the joining of the transcontinental railroad at Promontory Summit, Utah, on May 10, 1869. The photograph shows the two locomotives meeting, the workers crowded around, the dignitaries at the front. It's a technically excellent photograph for its era and widely reproduced.

The photographs I restore are not that famous. They're the photographs of the Chinese workers who built the western section of that railroad, the Irish workers who built the eastern section, the section foremen and engine crews and station masters who made the railroad run for the next 80 years.

Henry brought in a collection of photographs from his Chinese great-great-grandfather, who had survived the transcontinental railroad construction and settled in San Francisco. The photographs — formal studio portraits made after completion of the railroad — showed men who had made history that history had largely overlooked.

## Railroad Photography Across the Eras

Railway photography was technically ambitious from the beginning. Capturing moving trains required shorter exposures than the photography of the 1860s-1880s could easily manage, which drove early technical innovation. Crews and facilities, photographed stationary, were easier — and produced the archive we most often work with.

**Construction photographs** from the 1860s-1880s document the building of American infrastructure. These are often wet-plate collodion works — glass plate negatives that have survived in archives and occasionally in family collections.

**Crew portraits** across the steam era (1860s-1950s) document the workforce that operated the railways. These are generally formal group portraits with the visual conventions of occupational photography.

**Station documentation** — many railway companies photographed their stations, facilities, and equipment systematically. These photographs occasionally end up in family collections when workers received copies.

## Chinese Railroad Worker Photography

Photographs of Chinese railroad workers from the 1860s-1880s are rare. The systematic exclusion of Chinese workers from the official historical narrative included photographic documentation — they were frequently absent from the ceremonial photographs, despite doing a disproportionate share of the dangerous work.

Family photographs like Henry's — formal studio portraits of Chinese workers in post-railroad San Francisco — are a corrective record. Restoration of these photographs is part of the broader project of recovering a history that was deliberately minimized.

Henry's photographs came back with faces clearly identifiable. He donated high-resolution copies to the Chinese Historical Society of America.
