# Restoring Family Photos After an Apartment Fire

The fire in Jennifer's apartment started from a kitchen appliance at 3 AM. By the time the fire department arrived, the kitchen was destroyed. The living room — where her family photographs were displayed and stored — had sustained severe smoke damage but hadn't burned. The fire department's water had done additional damage.

Three damage sources, then: direct heat (in the kitchen area where some photographs were stored), smoke contamination (throughout the apartment), and water from firefighting.

## Assessing Fire-Adjacent Photograph Damage

**Heat damage without burning** creates a characteristic pattern. Photographs exposed to intense heat but not flames may show:
- Warped or melted backing
- Emulsion blistering or separation
- Color shift from thermal dye breakdown
- Brittleness from paper dehydration

These photographs are fragile. Handle with extreme care. Scan before attempting any physical treatment.

**Smoke and soot** deposits on photographic surfaces block the image. The soot is carbon-based and sits on top of the emulsion rather than in it — this means it can be removed physically, though doing so safely requires proper materials and technique.

**Firefighting water** with fire retardant chemicals creates complex contamination. Rinse with clean water and allow to air dry fully before scanning.

## What Can Be Saved

Jennifer's collection divided roughly into thirds. One-third were in good enough condition that restoration would bring them back fully. One-third had sustained sufficient damage that restoration would improve them but not fully recover them. One-third were too severely damaged for restoration.

The photographs she cared most about were, by luck, among the recoverable ones — stored in a fireproof box she'd bought years before and never expected to need.

**Smoke-stained photographs:** After careful physical cleaning (soft brush to remove loose soot, not wiping which can grind particles into the emulsion), these scan well. AI restoration addresses the remaining haze effectively.

**Heat-damaged photographs:** Where the emulsion has blistered or separated, the underlying image may be partially intact. Careful scanning captures what survives; AI fills in what's been lost with varying accuracy.

For photographs after a fire, the message I consistently deliver: don't throw anything away until you've scanned it. Even badly damaged photographs may contain recoverable information.
