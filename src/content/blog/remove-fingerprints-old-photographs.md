Fingerprints are one of the most insidious forms of photograph damage because they look minor but become permanent over time. Skin oils chemically react with emulsion and dyes, etching marks into the photo surface. This guide explains how to handle fingerprint damage safely.

## Why fingerprints damage photos

Skin oils contain:
- **Fatty acids**: react with silver gelatin emulsion
- **Sweat (salt + acids)**: corrodes silver layer over time
- **Microscopic skin debris**: bonds with emulsion

Three damage stages:
- **Hours-days**: fingerprint visible but reversible
- **Weeks-months**: fingerprint etching begins, partially recoverable
- **Years**: permanent chemical damage to physical print

For old photos with year-old or older fingerprints, physical cleaning won't reverse the etching. AI digital restoration on a scan is the path forward.

## Why physical removal usually fails

Skin oils smear when wiped:
- Light wipe → spreads oil to clean areas
- Solvent → dissolves emulsion or dyes
- Eraser → abrasion damage to image
- Water → emulsion softening + edge damage

The exception: **fresh fingerprints** (within days) sometimes lift with a clean dry microfiber cloth in light parallel passes. Test on edge first.

For everything else: scan and rely on AI restoration.

## Glove protocol going forward

To prevent further fingerprint damage:
1. **Cotton gloves** for general handling
2. **Nitrile gloves** for damp or oily contact
3. **Edges only** — never touch the image surface
4. **Both hands** support full photo during movement
5. **Clean workspace** — eat/drink away from photo collection

Most family historians don't know this until they see fingerprint damage on an irreplaceable photo. Move all family photos to gloves-only handling protocol from now on.

## Storage to prevent fingerprints

- **Archival polyester sleeves** (one photo per sleeve, no contact between prints)
- **Acid-free archival boxes** (contains sleeves, blocks dust)
- **Stable environment** (65–70°F, 30–50% RH)
- **Annual inspection** to catch new damage early
