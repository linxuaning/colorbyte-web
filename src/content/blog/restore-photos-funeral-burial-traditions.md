Funeral and burial photographs exist in every culture, though the conventions of documentation differ significantly. American funeral photography ranges from the post-mortem photography of the Victorian era to the contemporary practice of photographing funerals as memorial documentation.

## Cultural Funeral Photography

Different cultural traditions approach funeral photography differently. Mexican Día de los Muertos creates a specific photographic context that combines celebration and memory. New Orleans second-line funerals create a joyful photographic context. Traditional Chinese funerals and Jewish funerals each have specific documentary conventions. Restoration serves all of these.

## The Memorial Photograph Function

Photographs taken at funerals serve a specific memorial function: they document who was present to honor the deceased, the ceremonial context of the death, and the community of grief. This documentation has archival value independent of its emotional weight.

## Sensitivity in Restoration

Funeral photographs require the same technical restoration as any other photographs, but the context demands sensitivity to what the family needs. The restoration standard should serve the memorial purpose: clear, dignified, honest.

## Getting the Best Results

Start with the highest-quality scan you can produce — 600 DPI minimum for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small prints or photographs with faces you want to identify. Color mode scanning, even for black-and-white photographs, gives AI restoration algorithms more information to work with.

After restoration, compare the result with the original at full zoom. Check faces carefully to ensure identity is preserved, and note any areas where AI may have filled in damaged sections with plausible but uncertain reconstructions.
