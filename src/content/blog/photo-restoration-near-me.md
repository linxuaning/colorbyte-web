## What Local Photo Restoration Shops Actually Do

Local photo restoration services — usually found at print shops, framing stores, photography studios, or dedicated restoration businesses — typically offer:

**Manual digital restoration:** A skilled editor uses Photoshop to hand-repair damage. Scratches are cloned out manually. Fading is corrected with Curves and Levels. Faces are sharpened with masking and frequency separation techniques.

**Scanning:** Most local shops will scan the original print before restoring it. Better shops use flatbed scanners at 600–1200 DPI; some use drum scanners for valuable originals.

**Printed output:** Many local shops offer restored prints rather than just digital files, which is useful if you want a physical result.

## What Local Photo Restoration Costs

Pricing varies significantly by region and shop type, but typical ranges:

| Service | Typical Price |
|---------|--------------|
| Simple fading correction | $25–$50 |
| Moderate damage (scratches, tears) | $50–$100 |
| Severe damage (major tears, heavy water damage) | $100–$200+ |
| Face reconstruction on heavily degraded photo | $75–$150 |

For a typical 1950s–1970s family photo with moderate fading and light scratches, expect $50–$100 at most local shops. Complex work — significant physical damage, major face reconstruction — often exceeds $100.

Most local restoration services take **3–14 business days**. Shops that do hand restoration (Photoshop work) need time to work through their queue. Larger chain stores may send work to a central facility, adding days.

## When Local Shops Are Worth It

Local photo restoration is the better choice in specific situations:

**Extremely valuable or fragile originals:** A professional can assess physical condition and handle originals carefully. If you have a daguerreotype or wet plate collodion print from the 1800s that's deteriorating, physical conservation (not just digital restoration) may be needed. That's a conservator, not a print shop.

**You specifically need a printed output:** If you want a large print — 16"×20" or larger — from the restoration, a local lab with a high-end printer may produce better output than printing a file yourself at a retail kiosk.

**The damage is unusual or complex:** AI models are trained on common degradation patterns. Extremely unusual damage — chemical staining in specific patterns, major composite work on torn photos — may benefit from a skilled human editor's judgment.

**You want someone to handle the scanning:** If you're not comfortable scanning the original, some shops will scan it carefully and return the original.

## How to Get a Good Scan Before Using AI

If your photo is an original print, scanning it properly before uploading produces significantly better results than a phone snapshot.

**Phone photo (easiest):**
1. Lay the photo flat in even light — no shadows, no direct sunlight
2. Hold your phone directly above — no angle
3. Use the document scanner in Microsoft Lens (free) or Google PhotoScan
4. Export at maximum resolution

**Flatbed scanner (better quality):**
- 600 DPI minimum; 1200 DPI for small prints (wallet size or smaller)
- Many public libraries have free flatbed scanners
- Save as TIFF or high-quality JPEG
