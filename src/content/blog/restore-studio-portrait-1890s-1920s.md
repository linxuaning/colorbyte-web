The formal studio portrait was, for most of the period between 1860 and 1920, the primary photographic experience for ordinary Americans. You didn't own a camera. You went to a studio — once a decade if you were poor, once a year if you could manage it — and sat for a professional who knew how to use the machinery of light and chemistry to produce something lasting.

The results are among the technically finest photographs of their era. Professional photographers invested in the best equipment, the best chemistry, and the best papers. They had incentive to produce work that would last, because their reputation depended on satisfied customers.

Which makes it particularly interesting when these photographs arrive in poor condition. The best work of the best photographers of 1895 has sometimes been poorly stored, badly exposed to light, or simply suffered through 130 years of chemistry. Even quality deteriorates.

## The Cabinet Card as Object

The cabinet card (approximately 4.25 × 6.5 inches mounted on thick cardboard) dominated formal portraiture from the mid-1870s through approximately 1900. The format was designed to be collected and displayed — placed in card albums, arranged on parlor tables, sent to family members.

The cardboard mount is both the cabinet card's strength and its weakness. It provides physical stability for the photograph. But the mounting board itself is typically made from wood-pulp paper that's inherently acidic — the acid migrates into the photograph over decades.

**Recognizing mount acid damage:** Browning that's most concentrated at the edges of the photograph, gradually lighter toward the center. This is the migration pattern of acid from the mounting board.

## Specific Formats and Their Restoration

**Carte-de-visite** (visiting card photographs, 2.5 × 4 inches) were the predecessor to cabinet cards. They're smaller, which means smaller faces, which means higher-resolution scanning (1200 DPI minimum) for useful face enhancement.

**Cabinet cards** can be scanned at 600-800 DPI given their larger size. Check the mounting board for studio information on the back — this is often printed with studio name, city, and sometimes date, which provides essential context.

**Imperial portraits** (later 19th century large-format studio work) are larger still — sometimes 8 × 10 inches or more — and contain extraordinary detail when properly scanned.

## Restoration Goals for Formal Portraits

The formal studio portrait of 1890-1920 was made with specific aesthetic intentions: dignified composition, careful lighting, a sense of permanence. Restoration should honor these intentions.

**Tone correction** to remove the yellowing and recover the intended neutral-to-slightly-warm tonal palette of silver gelatin prints.

**Highlight recovery** for areas that have faded to pale gray — bringing back detail in skin, fabric, and backdrop.

**Conservative sharpening** — these portraits have an intentional quality of stillness and formality that aggressive sharpening disrupts.
