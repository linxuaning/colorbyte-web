# Restoring Old School Class Photos

Every school class photograph is simultaneously a document of individual lives and a portrait of a moment in American education. The children in the photograph are frozen at an age they occupied for exactly one year, in a school that may no longer exist, wearing clothes that date the image as precisely as a newspaper.

Tom's grandmother had saved every class photograph from her years at Franklin Elementary — third grade through eighth, 1931 through 1937. She could name every child in every photograph. Tom wanted them restored before the last person who could do that was gone.

## The Structure of the School Photo

School class photographs from the early-to-mid 20th century followed a consistent format: rows of children (standing in back, sitting in front), teacher or teachers at one end, photographed outdoors or in a gymnasium, with the school name and year printed below.

This format creates specific restoration challenges.

**Small faces.** In a class of thirty children photographed at sufficient distance to fit everyone in the frame, individual faces may be 50-100 pixels across in a standard scan. AI face enhancement works less well at this scale. The solution is high-resolution scanning: 1200 DPI for photographs where individual face detail matters.

**Exposure variation.** Outdoor group photographs in direct sunlight produce harsh shadows under noses and chins. Indoor photographs with inadequate flash coverage are often dark at the edges. Both conditions complicate face enhancement.

**Uniform damage.** School photographs were institutional productions, and they were often stored institutionally — stacked, folded, distributed to families who stored them with varying care. The damage patterns reflect this: many small scratches, edge wear, handling creases.

## Decade-by-Decade Technical Notes

**1920s-1930s:** Silver gelatin on fiber base paper. Generally stable if stored well, but chemical spotting and highlight fading are common. Black-and-white with characteristic cool tone.

**1940s-1950s:** Similar materials, but printing quality in school photographs specifically is often lower than professional studio work — rushed production, less careful processing.

**1960s-1980s:** The shift to color. Institutional school photography moved to color in the 1960s, with the familiar Kodak school portrait aesthetic. The color stability problems of consumer-era Kodacolor apply here too.

Tom's collection came through well. The faces in the 1930s photographs were sharp enough that Tom could circulate prints to the local historical society, which had records that helped identify several children he didn't know by name.
