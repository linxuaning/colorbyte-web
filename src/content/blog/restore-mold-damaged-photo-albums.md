You discover an old family album in your basement or attic. The cover is musty. Inside, the prints have white or grey fuzzy patches, brown stains where pages stuck together, and a smell that says "this album spent years in a damp environment". You want to restore these photos. AI can help — but only after you handle the mold first.

This guide walks through the combined manual + AI workflow for mold-damaged photo albums.

## Why AI alone can't fix mold

Mold on photographs is physical contamination — fungal growth on the print surface. AI restoration tools see mold as image content and may try to "preserve" it during enhancement. Running AI restoration on a mold-damaged scan typically produces:
- Mold patches sharpened along with the rest of the image
- AI guessing image content beneath the mold (often wrong)
- The "restored" version still showing mold artifacts

The correct workflow is: physical cleanup first, then scan, then AI restoration.

## Step 1: Triage your album

Not every mold-damaged photo justifies a full restoration. Sort photos into three categories:

**High priority** (worth full effort):
- Only-existing photos of deceased relatives
- Wedding or rite-of-passage portraits with no negatives
- Historic family group shots

**Medium priority** (worth modest effort):
- Multiple-copy photos where one copy is mold-damaged
- Background or context photos that contribute to a family narrative

**Low priority** (probably skip):
- Heavily-damaged prints with little remaining detail
- Photos with copies in better condition elsewhere
- Generic landscape or scenery shots not tied to specific family memories

Triage saves time and keeps the project tractable.

## Step 2: Mold cleanup — DIY vs professional

**For light surface mold** (small dry spots, easily brushed off):
- Wear cotton gloves to handle prints
- Use a soft natural-bristle brush to gently dislodge dry mold spots
- Work in a well-ventilated area or wear a dust mask
- After brushing, clean the print surface with a microfiber cloth
- Allow prints to fully dry in low-humidity conditions before scanning

**For severe mold** (active wet growth, multiple layers, paper structural damage):
- Stop and consult a professional photo conservator
- Disturbing wet mold can release spores (health concern)
- Severely-damaged prints may need acid-neutralization treatments
- Professional conservation runs $50-200 per print but preserves irreplaceable items

**For prints stuck together** (mold has glued pages):
- Don't force them apart — you'll tear the emulsion
- Professional conservators have humidity-chamber techniques to safely separate stuck prints
- This is generally beyond DIY scope

## Step 3: Scan the cleaned prints

After cleanup is complete and prints are fully dry, scan at high resolution (600-1200 DPI for standard prints, 2400+ for small contact prints). Use a clean flatbed scanner.

Save scans as TIFF or PNG (uncompressed). Include the original cleaned print in archival storage — even after digital scanning, the physical artifact remains the canonical historical record.

## Step 5: Document and archive

For mold-damaged restoration, documentation matters more than usual:
- Note the original mold severity and cleanup method used
- Save before/after scans for transparency
- Add metadata: "Print had surface mold, cleaned [date], scanned [date], AI-restored [date]"
- Archive both the original cleaned print AND digital scans

This documentation is valuable for genealogical research because future researchers will see "this photo had mold history" and understand any minor remaining artifacts.

## Special case: mold-affected color slides

Mold on color slides is rarer (cool, dry conditions are typical for slide storage) but does occur. The cleanup approach differs:
- Don't brush color slides with bristles — emulsion is delicate
- Use compressed air to dislodge dry spores
- For wet mold, a gentle wipe with a microfiber cloth dampened with distilled water (test on a non-essential slide first)
- Allow to dry completely before scanning

After cleanup, AI restoration handles slides similarly to prints.

## Realistic expectations

Mold-damaged photo restoration is one of the harder restoration scenarios. Even with perfect cleanup, some prints have permanent damage to the silver image layer beneath the mold (the chemicals from active mold growth can react with silver halide). For those prints, AI restoration improves the appearance but cannot make the photo "as new".

For irreplaceable photos with severe damage, the goal of restoration shifts from "perfect recovery" to "best possible interpretation" — which is still valuable for sharing the image with descendants.

## Final note

A mold-damaged photo album is not a lost cause. With careful triage, manual cleanup, and AI restoration of the cleaned scans, most albums produce usable family-history records. The combined workflow respects the limits of each approach: AI for what AI does well, human conservation for what AI can't substitute.
