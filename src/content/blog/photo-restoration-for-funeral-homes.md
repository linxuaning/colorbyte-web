Few professional services carry as much emotional weight as helping a grieving family create a dignified memorial display for a lost loved one. ## Why Is Memorial Photo Restoration a Natural Funeral Home Service?

Funeral homes already manage every other element of memorial services: venue, arrangements, flowers, programs, and displays. Photo services are a natural extension that most families need and that most funeral homes currently outsource or cannot offer at all.

The typical scenario: a family comes in to arrange a service with several photographs to consider for display. One or two are the right subject matter but in poor condition. Previously, the options were to use the damaged photo as-is, find a separate photo restoration vendor with an uncertain timeline, or go without.

With AI tools and basic scanning equipment in-house, the answer is: "We can restore and enlarge that for you. It will be ready for the service."

## What Does the In-House Workflow Look Like?

A funeral home photo restoration workflow is faster than most imagine. Here is the realistic sequence:

**Intake** (5 minutes): The family brings physical photographs. Examine for the best candidate — clearest facial detail, least damage, best pose. If multiple candidates exist, scan all of them.

**Scanning** (5-10 minutes): Place the photograph on the flatbed scanner. Scan at 600-1200 DPI depending on print size. Save the scan.

**AI restoration** (5-10 minutes): Upload to [Old Photo Restoration](/old-photo-restoration). The **Real-ESRGAN** upscaling and **NAFNet** denoising pipeline runs in under 90 seconds. Review the output at 100% zoom. For portraits, add a [Photo Enhancer](/photo-enhancer) pass for facial detail — another 90 seconds.

**Review** (5 minutes): Show the family the enhanced version on a screen if possible, or proceed directly if timing is tight. Flag any areas that still need manual touch-up.

**Print order** (varies): Send to in-house printer (15-20 minutes for an 8x10-11x14) or to a local lab for larger formats.

Total staff time: 20-35 minutes per photograph. Total cost at $4.99 for ArtImageHub's tools and $2-5 for printing materials.

## How Do You Handle Different Size Requirements?

Memorial displays use a range of sizes depending on the venue and display context:

**8x10**: Standard easel portrait, appropriate for smaller venues and home services.

**11x14**: Common for funeral home chapel displays. Visible from 10-15 feet.

**16x20**: Standard large portrait easel size. Most impactful for chapel or venue use. Requires input image of at least 1600x2000 pixels for acceptable print quality — achievable with AI upscaling from a decent original.

**20x24 and larger**: Reserved for significant installations or large venues. Requires strong original or very good AI upscaling from high-quality input. Most funeral homes order these from professional print labs.

AI upscaling specifically enables enlargements that would not be possible from the unprocessed scan. A 4x6 snapshot scanned at 600 DPI produces a file around 2400x3600 pixels — which AI upscaling can potentially double to 4800x7200, sufficient for an 11x14 or even 16x20 print at acceptable quality.

## Are There Situations That Require Manual Retouching?

Most memorial portrait restorations require no manual work after AI processing — the tools handle standard aging, grain, and fading comprehensively. A small percentage of cases benefit from targeted manual retouching:

**Significant physical damage** (tears through the face, large water-stained areas covering the subject) may need Photoshop work to reconstruct plausible detail. This is labor-intensive and should be priced accordingly.

**Multiple photographs composited** (combining two photographs to show both spouses clearly, or adding a recently deceased person to a group photo where they were not present) is a common request that requires Photoshop skills. Charge $75-150 for composite work.

**Photo colorization** requests — converting a black-and-white portrait to color — use the [Photo Colorizer](/photo-colorizer) tool for the initial colorized version, potentially with minor manual color adjustments. Charge $35-75 as an add-on.

## How Do You Introduce This Service to Families?

The simplest approach: make it part of your standard intake conversation. When families bring photographs during arrangement meetings, include a question: "If you'd like any of these photographs enhanced or enlarged for the service, we can do that. Would that be helpful?"

Most families have not thought to ask because they did not know the service existed. Offering it changes the dynamic from "making do with a damaged photo" to "having exactly the image we want displayed."

The families who use this service consistently cite it as one of the most meaningful things the funeral home provided. That word-of-mouth is worth more than the service revenue.

## How Do You Handle the Situation When the Only Available Photo Is Very Low Quality?

The most challenging funeral home photo service situation is when the only available photograph is very low resolution — a small Facebook profile picture, a blurry phone snapshot, or a years-old digital photo that was never high quality. AI enhancement can significantly improve these, but there are fundamental limits when the original information simply is not present. For very low-resolution phone photos (under 500 pixels in either dimension), the Photo Enhancer's SwinIR model will improve sharpness and reduce blocking artifacts, but cannot recover fine detail that was never captured. Set realistic expectations with families: show them the enhanced version before printing, confirm they are satisfied, and explain clearly that the result depends on the original quality. For extremely low-resolution originals, consider printing at smaller sizes where the quality holds — an 8x10 from a marginal original often looks acceptable, while a 20x24 from the same original will show visible pixelation regardless of AI enhancement. When a family has multiple photos available, scan all of them and compare the AI-enhanced results before selecting the best candidate for printing.
