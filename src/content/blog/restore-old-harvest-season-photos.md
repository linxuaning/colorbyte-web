## Why Does Harvest-Era Photography Fail in Predictable Ways?

The harvest field is one of the most technically demanding environments for photography, and it was especially so before the improvements in film latitude and lens quality that arrived in the postwar period. Direct overhead summer sun is maximum-contrast light: shadows under hat brims and equipment bodies go completely black, while straw, light-colored horses, and painted equipment surfaces blow out to featureless white. Early film technology — orthochromatic glass plates in the 1890s and 1900s, early nitrate film through the 1920s — had narrow exposure latitude that could capture either the highlights or the shadows clearly, rarely both.

The photographs that resulted are technically compromised at the source. Crew faces may be well-exposed while the threshing machine behind them blocks up in shadow. Or the photographer exposed for the equipment and the faces went gray and flat. Decades of subsequent storage — in grain office files, county extension archives, farm bureau records — added physical deterioration on top of the original photographic limitations.

## How Should You Scan and Prepare Harvest Photos for Restoration?

The scan quality ceiling determines the restoration quality ceiling. For prints larger than 4x6, 600 DPI captures sufficient information for high-quality restoration. For smaller prints — and harvest photos from the 1890s through 1910s were often small contact prints from glass plates — 1200 DPI is preferable. Save a lossless TIFF master from your scanner before converting to JPEG for upload. JPEG compression at scanning introduces artifacts that the restoration AI will work to remove along with the original degradation; starting from a clean TIFF master prevents this problem.

If the photo has emulsion cracking or significant curl, photograph it under even indirect light with a digital camera rather than scanning, which requires contact pressure that can worsen cracking. A camera at 45 degrees to the surface and bounced off two sheets of white foam board produces even illumination adequate for AI restoration.
