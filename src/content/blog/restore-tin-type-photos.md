# Restoring Tintype Photographs

Tintype photographs — also called ferrotypes — were made by coating a thin iron plate with a dark lacquer and a light-sensitive emulsion. Introduced in the 1850s and popular through the early 1900s, tintypes were the working-class portrait format of the 19th century: cheaper and more durable than paper prints, they could be made quickly by traveling photographers at fairs, carnivals, and markets. Families who have tintype photographs possess visual connections to people from the Civil War era or earlier, and these images deserve careful restoration.

## The History and Characteristics of Tintypes

Tintypes were enormously popular because they were inexpensive and nearly indestructible. Unlike daguerreotypes (which had polished silver surfaces requiring careful handling) or albumen prints (which required careful storage to prevent fading), tintypes could be bent, handled roughly, and stored in difficult conditions and still survive. This durability means many tintype collections have survived despite generations of careless storage. The images themselves have a distinctive aesthetic: relatively low contrast, with a gray-brown tonal range rather than the pure blacks and whites of well-made silver prints.

## Specific Tintype Damage Patterns

Despite their physical durability, tintypes suffer specific aging issues. Rust formation is the most significant: the iron base is susceptible to oxidation, and rust can spread across the image surface in reddish-brown patches that obscure image detail. The lacquer layer can crack, peel, or develop a network of fine cracks (crazing) that produces a visible pattern over the image. The emulsion layer may flake in areas of significant rust or physical damage. Scanning a tintype at high resolution and using AI restoration to address these specific damage types produces results that can dramatically clarify images that appeared nearly illegible.

## Scanning Tintypes: Special Considerations

Tintypes require specific handling when scanning because they can be magnetically attracted to scanner components and because they're often housed in decorative cases (similar to those used for daguerreotypes) that have their own preservation requirements. Scan with the scanner lid open or barely touching the surface, rather than pressing the tintype flat. Photograph tintypes under raking light (side illumination) before scanning to document any surface texture or damage that may not show in a standard scan. The resulting high-resolution scan provides the AI with sufficient detail to address even complex rust and surface damage patterns.
