## Yearbook Photo Damage Patterns

### Print Quality Issues

**Production-Related**:
- Mass printing compromises
- Paper quality variations
- Binding and handling wear
- Newsprint-style degradation

**Age-Related**:
- Yellowing of pages
- Fading of images
- Paper brittleness
- Foxing and spotting

### Handling Damage

**From Use**:
- Signing and writing on photos
- Highlighting or circling faces
- Page turning wear
- Spine stress on binding

**Storage Issues**:
- Humidity and temperature
- Stacking pressure
- Insect and rodent damage
- Attic and basement storage

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can small faces in class photos be enlarged clearly?**

Short answer: Yes, with some limitations. Our [AI upscaler](/ai-photo-upscaler) can significantly improve small faces in class photos, making individuals recognizable. Original print quality affects results—professionally printed yearbooks enhance better than cheaply printed ones. Expect good improvement, not perfection.

**How do I remove the dot pattern from printed yearbook photos?**

Short answer: AI restoration can reduce halftone dot patterns that appear when yearbook photos are enlarged. Complete removal isn't always possible, but visibility is typically improved significantly. Scanning at higher resolution and using our restoration tool together works best.

**Can yearbook photos with writing on them be restored?**

Short answer: The photo beneath writing can be enhanced, but writing itself won't disappear automatically. If the writing covers faces, AI may be able to reconstruct some detail. You may prefer keeping the writing as part of the memory—those signatures tell their own story.

**How do I find yearbook photos if I don't have the yearbook?**

Short answer: Check with the school district, which often maintains archives. Public libraries frequently have local yearbook collections. E-Yearbook.com and Ancestry.com have digital yearbook archives. Former classmates may share copies. Alumni associations sometimes have preserved collections.

**Should I restore individual photos or entire yearbook pages?**

Short answer: It depends on your purpose. Individual photos work best for sharing specific images or creating family archives. Entire pages preserve context—surrounding names, captions, and layout. Consider doing both: full page for archival purposes, individual extractions for specific use.
