Opening a storage unit after a flood and finding a box of family photographs is a specific kind of grief. The images might still be there — you can see shapes and faces — but the prints are stained, stuck together, mold-spotted, and warped. The question is whether anything can be saved.

The answer, in most cases, is yes. Not all of it. But more than you might expect.

This guide covers the physical stabilization steps first — the handling decisions that determine whether you preserve or destroy what survives — and then the digital restoration workflow that recovers the visible image from what you successfully scan.

## Why Does Flood Damage Look So Catastrophic — and Why Can AI Help?

Flood-damaged photographs look terrible because water affects several things simultaneously: it causes color dyes to bleed and fade, deposits minerals and organic material as it dries (the brown tide-mark rings), promotes mold growth on the gelatin emulsion layer, and can cause prints to bond together as the softened emulsion surfaces contact each other during drying.

Most of that damage is on the surface or in the color layer. The underlying image structure — the tonal information that gives you faces, shapes, and scenes — often survives better than the visible state of the print suggests.

This is why digital restoration works well on flood-damaged prints. When you scan the damaged photo, you capture everything the sensor can read — including image information beneath the staining. AI models like Real-ESRGAN and NAFNet then work on the tonal structure, correcting the contrast collapse and color shift that water damage causes, while inpainting tools reduce the staining artifacts. The result is often dramatically better than the physical print suggests is possible.

## What Physical Damage Is Recoverable vs Permanent?

Before you touch a single print, it helps to understand what you are actually dealing with.

**Recoverable with digital restoration:**
- Overall color fading and yellowing from chemical exposure to flood water
- Tide mark rings and water stain patterns (reduced, not always eliminated entirely)
- Surface mold bloom that has not eaten through the emulsion
- Low contrast and flat tonal range from chemical bleaching
- Overall softness and grain from emulsion layer disruption
- Color shifts from dye bleeding

**Permanent physical damage:**
- Sections where the emulsion layer has physically dissolved or detached, leaving bare paper
- Tears and missing sections where image material is gone
- Stuck prints where forced separation has removed emulsion from one or both prints
- Severe mold penetration that has consumed image structure rather than sitting on the surface

The practical implication: do not cause permanent damage trying to recover a print that could have been digitally restored from its damaged state. Forcing stuck prints apart is the most common cause of irreversible additional loss.

## How Should You Handle Wet or Damp Flood-Damaged Photos?

If you find the prints still damp, you have more options. Wet prints are easier to separate and less likely to suffer tearing during handling.

**Loose damp prints:** Lay each print face-up on clean paper towels or blotting paper in a well-ventilated area. Change the paper underneath as it saturates. Do not stack prints — they will stick to each other again. Keep out of direct sunlight and away from heat sources, which cause rapid uneven drying that warps and cracks the emulsion.

**Stuck damp prints:** Do not pull. Float the stuck pair face-up in a shallow tray of clean room-temperature water. The water penetrates the bonded surfaces slowly and allows separation with minimal force. Support both prints fully as they come apart — never let one half hang unsupported. Set each print face-up on clean paper to dry individually.

**Stuck dry prints:** This is the most difficult situation. If prints have dried fully bonded, do not attempt home separation. A paper conservator can use humidity chambers to re-soften the bond safely. If professional conservation is not accessible, scan the bonded pair as-is — you will capture whatever image comes through, and AI restoration can work with that. Forced dry separation almost always removes emulsion from one or both prints.

## How Should You Handle Mold-Affected Photos?

Mold on photographs is a real health concern as well as a preservation concern. Handle mold-affected prints outdoors or in a well-ventilated space, wear disposable gloves, and avoid touching your face.

For active mold (fuzzy, still growing), allow prints to dry completely — mold requires moisture to grow and will become dormant once the print is dry. Do not attempt to wipe or brush mold from a damp print; you will smear the mold deeper into the emulsion and cause additional damage. Once fully dry and mold dormant, gently brush the surface with a very soft brush in a ventilated area before scanning.

Mold staining — the dark spots left after the mold is gone — is permanent on the physical print. AI restoration reduces these artifacts in the digital copy but cannot fully eliminate severe staining that sits over important image areas.

## How Do You Scan Flood-Damaged Prints?

Once prints are fully dry and physically stable, scanning is the critical step. The scan becomes your permanent working copy — handle it with as much care as you would the original.

**Resolution:** 1200 DPI minimum for standard prints. For small-format originals (wallet size, contact prints, small snapshots), use 2400 DPI. Higher resolution gives AI models more pixel data to work with, which matters most when faces are small in the frame.

**Color mode:** Always scan in color, even for black-and-white photographs. Black-and-white prints often have warm or cool tones embedded in the paper chemistry, and scanning in color preserves this information.

**Flatbed scanner:** Use a flatbed scanner rather than a phone camera for best results. Phone camera photos of prints introduce additional distortion, uneven lighting, and focus issues that make restoration harder.

**File format:** Save scans as TIFF or high-quality JPEG. TIFF is lossless and preferred for archival purposes. Keep the original unmodified scan as your archival record — work from copies.

**Warped prints:** If a print has warped during drying, gently place it under weight (a heavy book on a clean sheet of paper) for 24 hours before scanning. Do not force a severely warped print flat — this can crack the emulsion. Scan it as flat as it will safely go and accept some edge distortion.
