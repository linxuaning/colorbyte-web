## Why Do Old Graduation Photos Deteriorate?

Understanding the damage helps you restore it effectively.

**Fading and yellowing** happen because photographic paper from the 1970s through 1990s used dye-based processes that were not designed for longevity. Exposure to light — even indirect light through a window — breaks down the dye molecules over time. The result is a photo that looks washed out, orange-shifted, or covered in a yellow cast.

**Tears and creases** are physical damage from handling, folding (photos stored in wallets), and accidents. These are the most visible type of damage and often what motivates families to seek restoration.

**Water damage** leaves tide marks, stains, and in severe cases causes the surface of the print to peel or separate. Floods, roof leaks, and spilled drinks are the most common causes.

**Foxing** — small brown or rust-colored spots — comes from mold and humidity interacting with the paper over decades. It's extremely common in photos stored in basements or non-climate-controlled areas.

**Artifact removal** handles foxing spots, scratches, and surface damage by identifying patterns that don't belong to the underlying image and removing them.

## What Results Are Realistic?

For lightly damaged photos — yellowing, minor foxing, small creases — AI restoration typically produces results that look nearly perfect. The damage disappears almost completely.

For moderately damaged photos — large water stains, significant tears, extensive foxing — restoration dramatically reduces the visible damage. Some evidence of the original damage may remain, but the photo becomes suitable for framing and display.

For severely damaged photos — large missing sections, faces obscured by damage, prints that have physically separated — AI restoration provides partial recovery. Faces and important subjects that are intact come through clearly; heavily damaged areas are reconstructed with varying accuracy.
