It happens when a photograph is framed without an archival mat: over time, humidity causes the photograph to adhere to the glass. Pulling the photograph away from the glass risks tearing the emulsion. Leaving it stuck leaves you with a photograph that can't be scanned.

If your photos are stuck to each other rather than to glass, use the separate guide to [photos stuck together](/blog/fix-photos-stuck-together). Glass-stuck prints usually need a different, more conservative handling path.

## Why Photos Stick to Frame Glass

The mechanism is straightforward: photographic gelatin and glass form an adhesive bond under repeated humidity cycling.

Photographic prints — both black-and-white and color — have a gelatin emulsion layer on their surface. Gelatin is hygroscopic: it absorbs atmospheric moisture when humidity rises and releases it when humidity drops. This swelling and contraction is small — a few micrometers — but over decades of cycling, the emulsion slowly flows into microscopic surface irregularities on the glass.

The result is a mechanical bond between the emulsion and the glass surface. It is not a chemical adhesion; there is no glue involved. It is a physical interlocking of the two surfaces at a microscopic scale.

**Three conditions accelerate this process:**
- No archival mat — the print rests directly on the glass with no air gap
- Seasonal humidity variation — summer humidity swells the emulsion; winter dryness contracts it
- Long framing duration — a photograph framed for 30-40 years has experienced hundreds of humidity cycles

Older black-and-white fiber-based prints and chromogenic color prints from the 1970s-1990s are most susceptible. RC (resin-coated) papers from the 1980s onward are somewhat less prone because the polyethylene layer provides a partial barrier.

## Assessment: Scan In Place First

Before attempting any physical separation, photograph or scan the print through the glass. This gives you a reference image regardless of what happens next. The glass creates some glare challenges, but shooting at an angle eliminates most of it.

**For photographing in-frame:** Set your camera or phone at a 20-30 degree angle to the glass surface. This moves specular reflections off the photograph's face. Use window light from the side rather than direct flash. Take multiple shots from slightly different angles and pick the clearest.

**For flatbed scanning:** Remove the photograph from the wall and place it face-down on the scanner glass. The photograph's glass is now face-to-face with the scanner glass — scan at 600 DPI with the scanner lid fully open (not pressing down). The scanner's own light source will work through both glass surfaces. Results are often better than photographing because there are no reflection angles to manage.

## Safe Separation Techniques

If the photograph must be separated from the glass, consult a professional conservator rather than attempting it at home. The risk of tearing emulsion is significant, and the techniques that work safely require professional equipment.

**What conservators do:** A trained conservator typically uses a controlled humidity environment — a sealed chamber where relative humidity is raised slowly to 70-80% — to re-swell the emulsion slightly and reduce the mechanical bond. They then use thin mylar strips or micro-spatulas to work the photograph free millimeter by millimeter.

**What not to do at home:** Running water over the frame, using steam, applying solvents, or pulling the photograph from a corner. Each of these risks tearing the emulsion or causing water damage to the print. The bond is at the emulsion surface; any technique that forces rather than releases the bond risks taking the emulsion with the glass.

For most family photographs, the scan-in-place approach produces a high-quality digital copy without risking the original. The original can remain in its frame indefinitely.

## What the Glass Texture Transfer Looks Like

When a photograph has been stuck to glass for many years, the glass surface leaves a pattern in the emulsion — called texture transfer. This appears as a fine grid or stipple pattern pressed into the surface of the photograph, most visible in highlights and lightly toned areas.

The severity of texture transfer varies with the glass type. Plain float glass produces a nearly invisible fine texture. Textured decorative glass (popular in picture frames through the 1980s) leaves a visible pattern that can significantly obscure fine detail.

AI restoration handles texture transfer moderately well. The algorithms recognize surface texture patterns as distinct from image detail and apply texture reduction that makes the pattern less prominent. Results improve significantly when:
- The texture is fine and uniform (plain glass)
- The areas beneath the texture contain fine detail that the AI can reference
- The scan is at high resolution (1200 DPI) giving the algorithm more information

Areas of flat tone — uniform skies, plain backgrounds — show more residual texture than detailed areas after restoration because there is less reference detail available.

## After Recovery

## Getting the Best Results

Start with the highest-quality scan you can produce — 600 DPI minimum for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small prints or photographs with faces you want to identify. Color mode scanning, even for black-and-white photographs, gives AI restoration algorithms more information to work with.

After restoration, compare the result with the original at full zoom. Check faces carefully to ensure identity is preserved, and note any areas where AI may have filled in damaged sections with plausible but uncertain reconstructions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I separate a photo stuck to glass without professional help?

For irreplaceable photographs, professional conservation is strongly recommended. A conservator uses controlled humidity chambers and micro-spatulas to release the adhesion without tearing the emulsion. For less critical photographs, the scan-in-place approach described above lets you digitize the image without risking physical separation at all.

### What causes photos to stick to frame glass?

Photographs stuck to glass almost always have two contributing factors: direct glass contact (no archival mat to create a gap) and humidity cycling. The gelatin emulsion on photographic paper swells slightly when humid and contracts when dry. Repeated cycles over years cause it to flow microscopically into the glass surface and bond to it. Older black-and-white prints and chromogenic color prints from the 1970s-1990s are most susceptible.

### Can AI restoration fix the glass texture pressed into a photo?

AI restoration can reduce the appearance of glass texture transfer — the pattern of microscopic surface markings from the glass pressed into the emulsion — but cannot fully eliminate it. The texture reduction works best when the pattern is fine and uniform. Coarser glass textures or cases where the emulsion was physically embossed leave residual marks.

### What DPI should I scan a glass-stuck photo?

Scan at 600 DPI for standard-size prints (4×6, 5×7). For smaller prints or photographs with faces you want to identify clearly, use 1200 DPI. Scanning through glass adds mild diffusion — the higher resolution compensates by giving AI restoration more information to work with. Always scan in color mode even for black-and-white photographs.
