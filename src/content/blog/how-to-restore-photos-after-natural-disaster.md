## What Should You Do With Wet Photos in the First Hour?

The 24-to-48-hour mold window is the single most important fact in disaster photo recovery. Wet photographic paper is an ideal growth medium for mold, and once mold colonies establish themselves in the emulsion, they begin permanently digesting the image. Your first priority is to interrupt that clock.

**Fan-dry, never heat-dry.** Lay wet photos face-up on a clean mesh screen or paper towels in a well-ventilated area. A box fan blowing gently across the surface accelerates drying without heat damage. Never use a hair dryer, clothes dryer, microwave, or direct sunlight — heat causes the emulsion to bubble, crack, or adhere to surfaces it touches, creating irreversible damage on top of the water damage.

**Separate before drying.** Wet prints stick together with alarming permanence as they dry. Gently float them apart in a tray of clean, cool water if they are already fusing. If they have dried fused, do not force them apart — scanning them in contact and using AI restoration to correct the overlap is a better outcome than tearing both prints.

**Freeze if you cannot dry immediately.** Place wet, separated photos in a single layer in zip-lock bags (interleave with wax paper to prevent adhesion) and freeze. Freezing halts mold growth completely, buying you days or weeks. Thaw slowly at room temperature when you are ready to proceed.

## How Do You Handle Smoke-Damaged and Heat-Warped Prints?

Wildfire and structural fire create a different damage profile than flood. Smoke deposits a layer of soot, ash, and volatile organic compounds across the emulsion surface, causing a brown-yellow cast, loss of contrast, and in severe cases, surface stickiness. Heat warping deforms the paper substrate and can partially melt the emulsion.

For smoke damage, the physical handling step is a single, light pass with a soft, dry artist's brush to remove loose soot. Do not wipe or use any liquid — soot smears into the emulsion and becomes permanent. After removing surface debris, scan the print at 1200 DPI minimum before any further cleaning. The scan preserves whatever detail remains in the degraded emulsion layer.

For heat warping that has not melted the emulsion, place the print face-down between two sheets of acid-free paper under a heavy, flat weight for 24 hours. Moderate warping often relaxes enough to scan flat. Severe warping that has fused or crazed the emulsion surface cannot be physically reversed, but AI processing can still recover significant legibility from a scan of the damaged original.

## What Prioritization Framework Works When You Have Hundreds of Photos to Rescue?

After a major disaster, a household may have 200 to 1,000+ photographs in various states of damage. Attempting to treat every photo equally in the emergency window guarantees that the most important ones don't receive attention in time. Use a four-tier triage system:

**Tier 1 — Irreplaceable originals.** Photos with no negative, no digital copy, and no other known print. Pre-digital era family portraits, military service documentation, and photos of people who have since passed belong here. These receive attention first, no matter the damage severity.

**Tier 2 — Active damage risk.** Any photo that is still wet, showing early mold fuzz, or at risk of adhesion to another print. Damage actively compounds with each passing hour; stop the bleeding before addressing lower tiers.

**Tier 3 — Chronologically significant.** Wedding albums, milestone birthday or anniversary events, graduation and school photos, immigration and settlement documentation. High meaning, but usually the damage is stable by this point.

**Tier 4 — Duplicates and casual snapshots.** Any photo where at least one other copy exists. Address these only after Tiers 1–3 are physically stabilized and digitized.

## Why Should You Digitize Before Returning to Physical Storage?

Physical stabilization is not the endpoint — it is the halfway point. A photo that has survived a flood and been carefully dried can still be destroyed by the next pipe leak, the next wildfire season, or simple continued deterioration. Digitization at 1200 DPI or higher converts each stabilized photo into a lossless digital asset that can be backed up in multiple geographic locations simultaneously.

Scan in color mode even for black-and-white originals. Color scanning captures the full tonal range of the image and gives AI restoration models more information to work with. Save as TIFF for archival master files and JPEG for sharing copies.

For insurance documentation, organize your digital archive with descriptive file names — for example, `1958-smith-family-christmas-living-room.tif` — and store copies both in cloud storage (Google Photos, iCloud, or Backblaze) and on a local external drive kept off-site. Adjusters respond well to organized, clearly labeled documentation.
