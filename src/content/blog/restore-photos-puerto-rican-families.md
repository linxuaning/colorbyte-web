## Island Photography Before and After Jones Act

Photographs taken in Puerto Rico before the Jones Act (1917) and the early decades of citizenship document a society in significant transition — from Spanish colonial rule to American territory status. Photography practices in Puerto Rico reflected both Spanish colonial traditions (formal studio portraits, Catholic ceremony documentation) and American influences that arrived after 1898. Photographs from this period often have a distinctive aesthetic that differs from both mainland American photography of the same era and contemporary Latin American photography.

## The Great Migration North and Its Documentation

The mass migration of Puerto Ricans to New York City from the 1940s through 1960s was documented in a distinctive photographic genre: the 'before departure' photo taken on the island with the whole extended family, and the 'after arrival' photos showing the new apartment in the Bronx or Spanish Harlem. These 'migration portrait' pairs are among the most historically significant photographs in Puerto Rican family archives, documenting both the social world that was left behind and the new life being built in America.

## Bomba, Plena, and Cultural Celebration Photography

Puerto Rican cultural life is inseparable from music and dance — bomba, plena, salsa — and the photographs of these cultural expressions document both community joy and cultural resilience. Photographs of neighborhood street dances, community cultural organizations, and the annual Puerto Rican Day Parade (which began in 1958 in New York) document the cultural life that Puerto Rican families built in their new communities. These images are of historical significance to the broader story of Caribbean culture's transformation of American urban life.
