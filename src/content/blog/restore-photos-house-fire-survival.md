The physics of house fires are relevant to understanding what photographs survive. Fire temperature, oxygen availability, and the thermal properties of photographic materials all determine survival. Understanding these factors helps set realistic expectations for recovery.

## What Physical Conditions Allow Survival

Photographs survive house fires most often when: they were stored in fireproof containers (obvious but effective), they were in a location where fire didn't reach, or they were in the center of a stack that carbonized on the outside but survived in the interior. Metal filing cabinets and safes are the most effective common storage containers.

## Surface vs. Internal Damage

Fire damage to photographs ranges from complete destruction to surface damage to heat discoloration to smoke deposit. Surface damage and heat discoloration are addressable through AI restoration; complete destruction is not. The gradient between these conditions means even apparently destroyed photographs sometimes contain recoverable information.

## Recovery Priorities

After fire damage, the priority for photograph recovery is: physically intact photographs first (scan immediately before additional damage), then smoke-damaged intact photographs (after safe physical cleaning), then heat-damaged photographs (scan and assess for digital restoration), then partially destroyed photographs (scan what remains).

## Getting the Best Results

Start with the highest-quality scan you can produce — 600 DPI minimum for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small prints or photographs with faces you want to identify. Color mode scanning, even for black-and-white photographs, gives AI restoration algorithms more information to work with.

After restoration, compare the result with the original at full zoom. Check faces carefully to ensure identity is preserved, and note any areas where AI may have filled in damaged sections with plausible but uncertain reconstructions.
